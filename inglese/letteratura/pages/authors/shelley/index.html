Mary Shelley

London, 1797 · London, 1851 — Creator of the modern monster

Section 01

Life — the essentials

1797
Born — London
Born Mary Wollstonecraft Godwin to two radical intellectuals: philosopher William Godwin and feminist writer Mary Wollstonecraft, who died ten days after Mary's birth. This loss of her mother at birth would shape Shelley's preoccupation with creation and abandonment.
1814
Elopement with Percy Bysshe Shelley
Aged 16, eloped to Europe with the Romantic poet Percy Bysshe Shelley, who was already married. She was accompanied by her stepsister Claire Clairmont. The couple lived in exile, experiencing poverty, scandal and the deaths of two children before 1816.
1816
The ghost story competition — Villa Diodati
The "Year Without a Summer" (volcanic winter from Mount Tambora's eruption). Lord Byron, Percy Shelley, Mary and John Polidori stayed at Villa Diodati on Lake Geneva. Byron proposed a ghost story competition. From a waking dream, Mary conceived Frankenstein.
1818
Frankenstein published — aged 20
Published anonymously (presumed to be Percy Shelley's work). Full title: Frankenstein; or, The Modern Prometheus. The subtitle signals the classical parallel: Prometheus stole fire from the gods and was punished — Victor Frankenstein steals the secret of life.
1822
Percy Shelley drowned — aged 29
Percy drowned in a sailing accident off the Italian coast. Mary was 24. She spent the rest of her life editing and promoting his poetry, and raising their surviving son Percy Florence.
1823–1851
Later life and revised Frankenstein
Published under her own name from 1823. Wrote The Last Man (1826, a post-apocalyptic novel) and Lodore (1835). The 1831 revised edition of Frankenstein — the version most readers know — added the preface explaining its origin. Died of a brain tumour in 1851.

Key vocabulary

Gothic novel
A literary genre combining horror, romance, supernatural elements and psychological terror — typically set in gloomy, isolated locations.
Prometheus
Greek Titan who stole fire from the gods to give to humanity; punished eternally. Shelley uses him as a parallel for Frankenstein's transgression.
The Double / Doppelgänger
A figure who mirrors or completes the protagonist — Frankenstein and his creature are each other's dark double.
Hubris
Greek term for excessive pride and ambition that leads to destruction — Frankenstein's defining flaw.
