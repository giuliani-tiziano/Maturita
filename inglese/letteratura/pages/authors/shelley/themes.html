Mary Shelley · Themes & Style

Themes & Style

Science · Responsibility · The double · Nature vs technology

Theme 01

The dangers of unchecked scientific ambition

Written in the age of the Industrial Revolution and Galvani's experiments with electrical current in dead frogs, Frankenstein is the first science fiction novel precisely because it takes a plausible scientific premise (reanimation through electricity) and asks: what if it worked?

Victor's error is not scientific curiosity itself — it is his refusal to consider the ethical implications. He asks "Can I do this?" but not "Should I do this?" This question resonates across centuries: from nuclear weapons to genetic engineering to AI.

Victor Frankenstein — the irresponsible creator

Victor's crime is not creating life — it is abandoning what he has created. He takes no responsibility for the creature's education, socialisation or wellbeing. The creature murders not from innate evil but from the agony of rejection. The lesson: creation demands responsibility.

"How dangerous is the acquirement of knowledge, and how much happier that man is who believes his native town to be the world, than he who aspires to become greater than his nature will allow." — Victor Frankenstein
Theme 02

Nature vs nurture — is the creature innately evil?

The creature's education

The creature is born innocent — like Locke's tabula rasa (blank slate) — and educated by observation and reading. He develops language, empathy and moral understanding. He saves a drowning girl, collects wood for the De Lacey family, feels joy at beauty. He is capable of love. It is rejection that transforms him.

Shelley's argument is that evil is not innate — it is produced by exclusion, loneliness and the denial of recognition. This is a profoundly Romantic and progressive argument: society, not nature, creates the monster. The creature explicitly compares himself to Adam (innocent) and to Satan (fallen through rejection).

Style

Literary style

  • Gothic elements — wild landscapes, Alpine storms, night experiments, the "hideous" and the sublime
  • Frame narrative — three nested voices (Walton / Victor / Creature) create epistemological uncertainty: whose version is true?
  • Epistolary opening — Walton's letters create the illusion of documentary reality
  • The Romantic sublime — nature as an overwhelming force that dwarfs and humbles the individual

Intertextual references

  • Milton, Paradise Lost — the creature as fallen Adam or Satan; Victor as God
  • Prometheus — the subtitle; fire of life stolen from divine realm
  • Goethe, The Sorrows of Young Werther — the creature reads this and identifies with the suffering romantic outsider
  • Rousseau — the "natural man" uncorrupted by society — a key source for the creature's characterisation
"Did I request thee, Maker, from my clay / To mould me Man?" — John Milton (epigraph to Frankenstein)
