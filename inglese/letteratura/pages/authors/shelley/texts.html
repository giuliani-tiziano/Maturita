Text 01

Frankenstein — Chapter V: "The creation" (1818)

"It was on a dreary night of November that I beheld the accomplishment of my toils. With an anxiety that almost amounted to agony, I collected the instruments of life around me, that I might infuse a spark of being into the lifeless thing that lay at my feet. It was already one in the morning; the rain pattered dismally against the panes, and my candle was nearly burnt out, when, by the glimmer of the half-extinguished light, I saw the dull yellow eye of the creature open; it breathed hard, and a convulsive motion agitated its limbs.

How can I describe my emotions at this catastrophe, or how delineate the wretch whom with such infinite pains and care I had endeavoured to form? His limbs were in proportion, and I had selected his features as beautiful. Beautiful! Great God! His yellow skin scarcely covered the work of muscles and arteries beneath; his hair was of a lustrous black, and flowing; his teeth of a pearly whiteness; but these luxuriances only formed a more horrid contrast with his watery eyes, that seemed almost of the same colour as the dun-white sockets in which they were set, his shrivelled complexion and straight black lips."

Gothic atmosphere

  • "dreary night of November" — pathetic fallacy: nature mirrors Victor's transgression
  • "one in the morning" — nocturnal creation: life made in darkness, outside God's light
  • "spark of being" — electric metaphor; anticipates Prometheus bringing fire
  • "dull yellow eye" — immediately grotesque; beauty aspired to, horror achieved

The creator's horror

Victor had selected "beautiful" features — but assembled together they produce revulsion. The passage enacts the Gothic uncanny: the familiar (human features) made unfamiliar (yellow skin, watery eyes). Victor's exclamation "Beautiful! Great God!" captures the collision between intention and reality.

Most importantly: Victor immediately abandons the creature ("I rushed out of the room") — the original sin of the novel. The creature's monstrosity is not the issue; the abandonment is.

Text 02

Frankenstein — The creature's demand (Chapter 10)

"I expected this reception," said the daemon. "All men hate the wretched; how, then, must I be hated, who am miserable beyond all living things! Yet you, my creator, detest and spurn me, thy creature, to whom thou art bound by ties only dissoluble by the annihilation of one of us. You purpose to kill me. How dare you sport thus with life? Do your duty towards me, and I will do mine towards you and the rest of mankind. If you will comply with my conditions, I will leave them and you at peace; but if you refuse, I will glut the maw of death, until it be satiated with the blood of your remaining friends."

The creature's eloquence

The creature speaks with complex, formal English — far from the inarticulate monster of popular culture. His eloquence proves his humanity and intelligence. He argues with legal and moral precision: Victor has duties as creator; the creature has rights as creation. "Do your duty towards me" — a social contract argument.

Moral argument

The creature's core argument is that Victor is responsible for what he has created. The creature is not inherently evil — but Victor's rejection has made him dangerous. Victor created the conditions for the murders that followed. This is Shelley's implicit critique of parental and social abandonment — the creature is every rejected child, every social outcast.

"Beware; for I am fearless, and therefore powerful." — Mary Shelley, Frankenstein
