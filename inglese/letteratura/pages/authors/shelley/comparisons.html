Mary Shelley · Comparisons

Comparisons

Shelley vs Golding · Shelley vs Wilde · Shelley vs Orwell

Comparison 01

Shelley vs Golding — Where does evil come from?

Shelley — evil is produced by society

The creature is born innocent. He learns empathy, develops language and morality, feels joy at music and beauty. He becomes violent only after repeated, definitive rejection. Evil is the product of social exclusion, abandonment and the denial of recognition. The solution is responsibility: if Victor had loved his creation, the tragedy would not have occurred.

Golding — evil is innate

In Lord of the Flies, the children are from a civilised background with no history of rejection or abuse — yet they descend into murder spontaneously once removed from social constraints. Jack needs no abandonment or rejection to become a killer. Evil is inherent in human nature, not produced by social conditions.

Comparison 02

Shelley vs H.G. Wells — Science fiction pioneers

Frankenstein (1818) — the first science fiction novel

Uses a plausible scientific premise (galvanism / electrical reanimation) to explore ethical questions: What are the limits of science? What are the creator's duties? The science is a vehicle for moral and philosophical exploration.

H.G. Wells — The Island of Doctor Moreau (1896)

Doctor Moreau vivisects animals to make them human — again a scientist transgressing natural limits. But Wells is darker: Moreau has no conscience. Both novels anticipate 20th-century anxieties about genetic engineering, artificial intelligence and the ethics of science.

Comparison 03

Shelley vs Orwell — Technology and power

Shelley's warning

Victor creates a technology (reanimation) without ethical framework. The technology develops its own agenda — the creature escapes Victor's control and becomes an autonomous agent of destruction. The creator cannot control what he has made.

Orwell's parallel

Orwell's Party creates Newspeak — a political technology that will eventually outstrip its creators. As the telescreen network expands and Newspeak shrinks the vocabulary of dissent, the system acquires its own momentum. Both warn: beware of technologies that escape ethical governance.

"Nothing is so painful to the human mind as a great and sudden change." — Mary Shelley, Frankenstein
