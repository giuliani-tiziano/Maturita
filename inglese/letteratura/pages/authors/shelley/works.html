Mary Shelley · Works

Major Works

The founding text of science fiction — written at 18

Masterwork

Frankenstein; or, The Modern Prometheus (1818)

Plot summary

The novel has a frame narrative structure: Captain Walton, on an Arctic expedition, rescues Victor Frankenstein and records his story. Victor, a student of natural philosophy in Geneva, becomes obsessed with the secret of life. He assembles a creature from corpse parts and animates it with electricity. Horrified by what he has created, he abandons it.

The creature, articulate and sensitive, seeks acceptance but is rejected everywhere. He learns language and reads Milton's Paradise Lost, Goethe's Werther and Plutarch's Lives. Rejected by the De Lacey family whom he has secretly observed and befriended, he turns to revenge. He kills Victor's brother William, his friend Clerval, and finally his wife Elizabeth. Victor pursues the creature to the Arctic, where he dies on Walton's ship. The creature disappears into the frozen north.

Narrative frame structure

Frame 1
Walton's letters to his sister Margaret — the outermost frame
Frame 2
Victor Frankenstein's first-person narrative — the main story
Frame 3
The creature's autobiography — told within Victor's narrative

The subtitle: The Modern Prometheus

In Greek myth, Prometheus stole fire from the gods to give to humanity. Zeus punished him by chaining him to a rock where an eagle ate his liver daily, only for it to regenerate each night — eternal suffering. Victor Frankenstein steals the "fire" of life — and his punishment is equally eternal: the creature destroys everything he loves.

In the Romantic period, Prometheus was a hero — the rebel against divine tyranny. But Shelley's subtitle is ambivalent: Victor is both heroic (he transcends human limits) and hubristic (he should not have).

"I, the miserable and the abandoned, am an abortion, to be spurned at, and kicked, and trampled on." — The Creature, Frankenstein

The creature reads Paradise Lost

The creature finds a copy of Milton's Paradise Lost and reads it as a literal account of his own situation: "I often referred the several situations, as their similarity struck me, to my own. Like Adam, I was apparently united by no link to any other being in existence; but his state was far different from mine in every other respect. He had come forth from the hands of God a perfect creature, happy and prosperous... but I was wretched, helpless, and alone."

"I ought to be thy Adam, but I am rather the fallen angel." — The Creature, Mary Shelley's Frankenstein
