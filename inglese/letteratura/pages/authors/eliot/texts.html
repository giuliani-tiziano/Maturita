Text 01

The Waste Land — "The Burial of the Dead" (1922)

April is the cruellest month, breeding
Lilacs out of the dead land, mixing
Memory and desire, stirring
Dull roots with spring rain.
Winter kept us warm, covering
Earth in forgetful snow, feeding
A little life with dried tubers.
Summer surprised us, coming over the Starnbergersee
With a shower of rain; we stopped in the colonnade,
And went on in sunlight, into the Hofgarten,
And drank coffee, and talked for an hour.

Inverting Chaucer

The poem opens by inverting Chaucer's Canterbury Tales: "Whan that Aprill with his shoures soote / The droghte of March hath perced to the roote" — for Chaucer, April is the month of pilgrimage and new life. For Eliot, "April is the cruellest month" because spring forces painful reawakening. Winter was preferable: it kept people in numbness ("forgetful snow"). The modern world prefers comfortable anaesthesia to painful consciousness.

Fragmentation and language shift

Line 8 suddenly shifts to a specific, personal memory (Lake Starnberg near Munich) and then to coffee in the Hofgarten — concrete, mundane, European. This juxtaposition of the mythic ("breeding / Lilacs out of the dead land") and the trivial (coffee, an hour's conversation) without transition is the Modernist technique. No single voice; no explanation. The reader must work to make connections.

Text 02

The Love Song of J. Alfred Prufrock (extract, 1915)

Let us go then, you and I,
When the evening is spread out against the sky
Like a patient etherised upon a table;
Let us go, through certain half-deserted streets,
The muttering retreats
Of restless nights in one-night cheap hotels
And sawdust restaurants with oyster-shells:
Streets that follow like a tedious argument
Of insidious intent
To lead you to an overwhelming question...
Oh, do not ask, "What is it?"
Let us go and make our visit.

The anti-Romantic simile

"Like a patient etherised upon a table" — the first simile of the poem is deliberately anti-poetic. Where Keats would compare the evening to something beautiful, Eliot compares it to a surgical patient under anaesthetic: passive, numb, cut open. This single image announces the Modernist programme: beauty is dead; only clinical precision and irony remain. Ezra Pound praised it for cutting through Victorian vagueness.

The "you and I" split

Who is "you"? The reader, perhaps — but also Prufrock's divided self: the social self that must make the visit and the reflective self that cannot stop thinking. The "overwhelming question" is never asked — it is endlessly deferred. Prufrock is Hamlet reborn in an Edwardian drawing room: all awareness, no action. "Do I dare?" he asks — and does not.

"I have measured out my life with coffee spoons." — T.S. Eliot, The Love Song of J. Alfred Prufrock
