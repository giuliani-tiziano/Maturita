Comparisons

Eliot vs Wordsworth · Eliot vs Joyce · Eliot vs Beckett

Comparison 01

Eliot vs Wordsworth — Nature and the modern world

Wordsworth — nature heals

For Wordsworth, nature is a moral teacher, a source of consolation and growth. "Spots of time" in the natural world restore the soul. The natural landscape (Tintern Abbey, the daffodils, the Simplon Pass) provides a structure of meaning. Spring is a time of joyful renewal — "The Child is father of the Man."

Eliot — nature is cruel, April is cruellest

Where Wordsworth finds renewal in spring, Eliot finds cruelty: "April is the cruellest month, breeding / Lilacs out of the dead land." The natural cycle forces painful consciousness on people who prefer numbness ("Winter kept us warm"). Eliot's waste land is precisely the nature Wordsworth celebrated — but seen after WWI, stripped of consolation. The post-Romantic poet cannot recover Wordsworth's innocence.

Comparison 02

Eliot vs Joyce — The mythic method

The shared technique

In his essay "Ulysses, Order, and Myth" (1923), Eliot defined the mythic method: "the manipulation of a continuous parallel between contemporaneity and antiquity" — using ancient myth as a structural skeleton beneath a modern narrative. Joyce uses Homer; Eliot uses the Fisher King, Dante, Tiresias. Both use myth to give "a shape and significance to the immense panorama of futility and anarchy which is contemporary history."

Different results

Joyce's mythic method is structural and comic — the Homeric parallel in Ulysses is ironic and humane. Eliot's use of myth is more desperate — the Fisher King is not a comic parallel but a cry for healing that never comes. Joyce finds human comedy beneath the myth; Eliot finds spiritual desolation. Both published in 1922: the year of Modernism's high water mark.

Comparison 03

Eliot vs Beckett — Spiritual emptiness

Eliot — emptiness as spiritual crisis

The Waste Land mourns the loss of shared religious and cultural meaning. The fragments shored against ruins are precisely fragments — incomplete, disconnected. But Eliot ultimately found his answer in Anglo-Catholicism (1927 conversion). The Four Quartets (1943) express a hard-won spiritual peace. For Eliot, the waste land was a stage, not a permanent condition.

Beckett — emptiness as permanent condition

Beckett's characters also wait, also speak in fragments, also find no meaning. But where Eliot found God, Beckett found only "perhaps." Godot does not come. The silence at the end of Beckett's plays is not Eliot's "Shantih" (peace beyond understanding) but simply "I can't go on. I'll go on." The post-WWII world could not sustain even Eliot's qualified religious resolution.
