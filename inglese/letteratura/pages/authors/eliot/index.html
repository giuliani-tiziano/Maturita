T.S. Eliot

St Louis, Missouri, 1888 · London, 1965 — The poet of the Waste Land

Section 01

Life — the essentials

1888
Born — St Louis, Missouri, USA
Thomas Stearns Eliot born in St Louis to a distinguished New England family. His grandfather founded Washington University. Studied at Harvard (1906–09), then the Sorbonne in Paris, then Merton College, Oxford. His American origins and European education created the transatlantic perspective central to his work.
1915
Settled in England — marriage to Vivienne Haigh-Wood
Married Vivienne Haigh-Wood impulsively in 1915. The marriage was catastrophic: Vivienne suffered from severe mental illness; Eliot was overwhelmed by her condition and his own depression. The breakdown of this relationship informed The Waste Land.
1915
The Love Song of J. Alfred Prufrock
Published in Poetry magazine. Ezra Pound had championed Eliot's work and helped find publishers. Prufrock introduced modern English poetry to fragmented syntax, allusion, irony and the anti-heroic protagonist.
1922
The Waste Land
Published in The Criterion and as a book. 434 lines in five sections; draws on the Fisher King myth, Sanskrit texts, Dante, Shakespeare, Baudelaire and others. Ezra Pound cut the original manuscript by half. Widely considered the defining poem of English Modernism: a vision of post-war spiritual desolation.
1927
British citizenship and conversion to Anglicanism
Became a British subject and was baptised into the Church of England. His later poetry — including Ash Wednesday (1930) and the Four Quartets (1943) — reflects his Christian faith. He described himself as "classicist in literature, royalist in politics, and Anglo-Catholic in religion."
1948
Nobel Prize in Literature
Awarded the Nobel Prize "for his outstanding, pioneer contribution to present-day poetry." Also the Order of Merit. His play Murder in the Cathedral (1935) and the Four Quartets are his major late works.

Key vocabulary

Objective correlative
Eliot's critical concept: the set of objects, events or situations which evoke a particular emotion in the reader — the external formula for an internal state.
Fragmentation
The central formal technique of The Waste Land: multiple voices, languages and cultural references juxtaposed without explanation — mirroring post-war cultural collapse.
The Waste Land
Eliot's central image: modern secular civilisation as a spiritual desert, unable to find regeneration — drawn from Arthurian myth (the Fisher King's wounded land).
Allusion
Eliot's poetry demands an encyclopaedic reader: it references Dante, Ovid, Shakespeare, Upanishads, Chaucer simultaneously.
