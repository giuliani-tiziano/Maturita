T.S. Eliot · Works

Major Works

The Love Song of J. Alfred Prufrock · The Waste Land — defining Modernist poetry

Key Work I

The Love Song of J. Alfred Prufrock (1915)

Published in Poetry magazine, this poem introduced modern English poetry. The speaker — J. Alfred Prufrock, a middle-aged man paralysed by self-consciousness — fails to ask a question (probably a declaration of love, possibly a philosophical question) at a social gathering. The poem is an interior monologue: no events happen, only the paralysis of a man who cannot act because he is too aware of his own inadequacy.

"Do I dare / Disturb the universe? / In a minute there is time / For decisions and revisions which a minute will reverse." — T.S. Eliot, Prufrock

Anti-heroic irony

The poem begins with an epigraph from Dante's Inferno (spoken by Guido da Montefeltro in Hell). This frames Prufrock as a damned soul confessing. The title calls it a "love song" but there is no love and no song — ironic deflation. "I have measured out my life with coffee spoons" — the grandiose reduced to the domestic. Prufrock parodies heroic tradition: "No! I am not Prince Hamlet, nor was meant to be."

Key Work II

The Waste Land (1922)

The defining poem of Modernism — 433 lines in five sections, dedicated to Ezra Pound ("il miglior fabbro"). Written during and after WWI, edited by Pound from a much longer manuscript, it presents a fragmented vision of a spiritually desolate modern world through multiple voices, languages and allusions.

The Five Sections

I. The Burial of the Dead
"April is the cruellest month" — spring brings painful reawakening; fear of life and memory
II. A Game of Chess
Two scenes of loveless relationships: upper-class neurosis and lower-class pub conversation
III. The Fire Sermon
The Thames, sordid London, the Tiresias figure witnessing a joyless seduction
IV. Death by Water
Phlebas the Phoenician drowned — a 10-line lyric, the only still point in the poem
V. What the Thunder Said
The journey to Emmaus; "Shantih shantih shantih" — the Indian peace beyond comprehension

The mythic method

The poem draws on: the Fisher King legend (a wounded king whose impotence causes his land to become a waste); Jessie Weston's From Ritual to Romance; Frazer's The Golden Bough; the Grail legend; Tiresias the hermaphrodite prophet from Greek myth; the Tarot. These myths provide a structural framework beneath the fragmented surface. T.S. Eliot praised Joyce's use of the same technique in Ulysses as "a way of controlling, of ordering, of giving a shape and a significance to the immense panorama of futility and anarchy which is contemporary history."

"April is the cruellest month, breeding / Lilacs out of the dead land." — T.S. Eliot, The Waste Land
