T.S. Eliot · Themes & Style

Themes & Style

Fragmentation · Spiritual desolation · The objective correlative · Allusion

Theme 01

Fragmentation and the modern waste land

WWI destroyed the belief in progress, civilisation and meaning that had sustained European culture. Eliot's poetic response was fragmentation: no single voice, no continuous narrative, no resolved meaning. The Waste Land is built from disconnected fragments — voices, languages, quotations — that refuse to cohere. The famous final lines — "These fragments I have shored against my ruins" — name the method directly.

The modern world is a "waste land": spiritually barren, sexually joyless, historically disconnected from the myths and beliefs that once gave life meaning. London is compared to Dante's Hell: "I had not thought death had undone so many." The crowds crossing London Bridge are the dead.

The Fisher King and the Grail legend

From Arthurian legend: the Fisher King is a wounded king whose sexual wound has caused his kingdom to become barren — a "waste land." The only cure is the Grail knight asking the right question ("What ails thee?"). Eliot uses this myth to suggest that modern spiritual impotence is the cause of cultural desolation. The question is never asked; the Grail is never found; the land remains waste.

Theme 02

The objective correlative — Eliot's critical theory

Definition

From Eliot's essay "Hamlet and His Problems" (1919): "The only way of expressing emotion in the form of art is by finding an 'objective correlative'; in other words, a set of objects, a situation, a chain of events which shall be the formula of that particular emotion; such that when the external facts, which must terminate in sensory experience, are given, the emotion is immediately evoked."

In other words: don't say "I am sad." Describe the thing that made you sad — if chosen rightly, the reader will feel the emotion directly, without being told to.

This is the opposite of the Romantic lyric tradition (Wordsworth's "emotion recollected in tranquillity"). Where Wordsworth expresses the emotion directly, Eliot seeks an impersonal, technical means of evoking it. The poem becomes an object, not a confession. This aligns Eliot with the Imagists (Pound, H.D.) who demanded precise images over vague emotion. "The apparition of these faces in the crowd; / Petals on a wet, black bough" (Pound's "In a Station of the Metro") — pure objective correlative.

Style

Eliot's poetic technique

  • Allusion — The Waste Land quotes or alludes to: Dante, Shakespeare, Spenser, Marvell, the Bible, Upanishads, Wagner, Ovid, Nerval, and many more — all condensed and fragmented
  • Multiple voices — no single speaker; voices merge, contradict and overlap without transition
  • Free verse — varies line length and rhythm; uses blank verse echoes but breaks them
  • City as landscape — London as a dead city, a modern hell; the crowd as the undead
  • Juxtaposition — high culture and low culture placed side by side without mediation

Eliot vs the Romantics

Where Wordsworth trusted emotion ("spontaneous overflow of powerful feelings"), Eliot distrusted it — "Poetry is not a turning loose of emotion, but an escape from emotion." Where Wordsworth found consolation in nature, Eliot found a spiritual wasteland. Where Wordsworth's ideal was simplicity, Eliot's was density and allusiveness. The Modernist break with Romanticism is nowhere clearer than in Eliot's reversal of Wordsworth's most central claims.

"These fragments I have shored against my ruins." — T.S. Eliot, The Waste Land
