Text 01

The Ring-verse — "One Ring to rule them all"

Three Rings for the Elven-kings under the sky,
Seven for the Dwarf-lords in their halls of stone,
Nine for Mortal Men doomed to die,
One for the Dark Lord on his dark throne
In the Land of Mordor where the Shadows lie.
One Ring to rule them all, One Ring to find them,
One Ring to bring them all, and in the darkness bind them,
In the Land of Mordor where the Shadows lie.
— J.R.R. Tolkien, The Lord of the Rings

Structure and repetition

The verse has a clear descending structure: Three → Seven → Nine → One. The numbers create a hierarchy, with the One Ring subordinating all others. The threefold repetition of "One Ring to…" builds to an inevitable conclusion — dominance.

The final line is repeated identically at the end of both stanzas: "In the Land of Mordor where the Shadows lie." Repetition signals entrapment — there is no escape from this pattern.

The simplicity of evil

The verse is written in Dark Speech on the Ring itself — it appears only when the Ring is heated. Its power is expressed through the simplest, most concrete verbs: rule, find, bring, bind. Evil in Tolkien is not complicated — it is the single, monomaniacal will to control.

"Doomed to die" — only Men are mortal; their mortality makes them vulnerable to the Nine Rings' corruption. They become the Nazgûl (Ringwraiths) — neither living nor dead.

Text 02

The Riddle Game — The Hobbit, Chapter 5

"What have I got in my pocket?" [Bilbo] said aloud. He was talking to himself, but Gollum thought it was a riddle, and he was frightfully upset.

"Not fair! not fair!" he hissed. "It isn't fair, my precious, is it, to ask us what it's got in its nasty little pocketses?"

Bilbo seeing what had happened and having nothing better to ask stuck to his question. "What have I got in my pocket?" he said louder.

"S-s-s-s-s," hissed Gollum. "It must give us three guesseses, my precious, three guesseses."
— J.R.R. Tolkien, The Hobbit, Chapter 5 "Riddles in the Dark"

Wit vs Power — and Providence

The riddle game is a traditional Norse/Old English contest — it decides life or death. Bilbo cheats accidentally (it is not really a riddle but a question). Gollum's obsessive rule-following — "It must give us three guesseses" — is both comic and tragic: he cannot break from convention even to save himself.

Tolkien's point: Bilbo wins not through strength or cunning but through luck/Providence. The Ring came to Bilbo because it was "meant" to — a hint of the deeper purpose running through the narrative.

Gollum's language

Gollum's speech is characterised by repetition ("my precious"), the hissing "s" sounds suggesting corruption and serpentine unnaturalness, and the plural "we/us" — he speaks of himself in the plural because the Ring has split his personality. He is simultaneously Sméagol (the original self) and Gollum (the Ring-corrupted self).

This chapter first establishes Gollum as a figure of pity — ugly, pathetic, trapped — which is why Bilbo does not kill him. That act of mercy saves the world centuries later.

Text 03

Sam at Mount Doom — "I can carry you"

"Come, Mr. Frodo!" he cried. "I can't carry it for you, but I can carry you and it as well. So up you get! Come on, Mr. Frodo dear! Sam will give you a ride. Just tell him where to go, and he'll go."

Frodo looked at him with eyes wide with wonder. "What a fool I am," he said. "And I don't like heights. But if Sam will carry me, then I suppose I can carry the burden."
— J.R.R. Tolkien, The Return of the King

The ordinary hero at the epic moment

This is the emotional climax of the novel's friendship theme. Sam cannot carry the Ring — that is Frodo's burden alone (the weight of absolute power falls on the individual conscience). But Sam can carry Frodo — the weight of a broken friend is something any loyal person can share.

The plain, colloquial language ("Come on, Mr. Frodo dear! Sam will give you a ride") is characteristic of Sam. The greatest act of heroism in the novel is expressed in the simplest terms — no heroic diction, just love.

Literary technique: contrast

Tolkien uses contrast throughout: the formal, archaic language of kings and elves vs the plain English of hobbits. At this moment of supreme crisis, the plain language wins. It is more moving precisely because it is undecorated.

Compare with the Psalms and the New Testament, which Tolkien knew deeply — the same power in plain, direct speech at moments of profound meaning.
