Works · J.R.R. Tolkien

The Works

From the children's tale to the mythological epic

Work 01

The Hobbit (1937)

A comfortable hobbit named Bilbo Baggins is swept into an adventure by the wizard Gandalf and thirteen dwarves, led by Thorin Oakenshield. Their quest: to reclaim the dwarves' ancestral home, the Lonely Mountain (Erebor), from the dragon Smaug.

During the journey Bilbo finds a magic ring in the tunnels below the Misty Mountains — taken from the creature Gollum. That ring becomes the central object of The Lord of the Rings.

Key elements

The Shire
Bilbo's home — a rural idyll representing pre-industrial England.
Gollum
A corrupted hobbit-like creature, enslaved by the Ring for centuries. His famous riddle game with Bilbo: "What have I got in my pocket?"
The Riddle Game
Chapter 5 — wit vs power. Bilbo wins through luck ("What have I got in my pocket?") — Providence at work.
Smaug
The dragon guarding the treasure — greed, power, and the dragon-sickness (Thorin's corruption).

Tone and themes

The Hobbit begins as a children's adventure with a lighter, more whimsical tone than The Lord of the Rings. Key themes:

  • Courage — Bilbo's gradual discovery of unexpected courage.
  • Home and adventure — the tension between comfort and growth.
  • Greed — the dragon-sickness that corrupts Thorin.
  • Providence — luck and coincidence as divine guidance.
Work 02

The Lord of the Rings (1954–1955)

Tolkien's magnum opus. The Ring that Bilbo found is revealed to be the One Ring — forged by the Dark Lord Sauron to control all other rings of power. Frodo Baggins, Bilbo's nephew, must carry it to Mount Doom in the land of Mordor and destroy it.

Vol. I

The Fellowship of the Ring

Frodo leaves the Shire with three friends. In Rivendell (the Elven refuge), the Fellowship of nine is formed: Frodo, Sam, Merry, Pippin, Gandalf, Aragorn, Legolas, Gimli, Boromir. They attempt to cross the Misty Mountains but are driven through the mines of Moria, where Gandalf falls fighting the Balrog.

Vol. II

The Two Towers

The Fellowship breaks apart. Frodo and Sam continue alone towards Mordor, guided by Gollum. Aragorn, Legolas and Gimli aid the kingdom of Rohan against Saruman's armies. The siege of Helm's Deep.

Vol. III

The Return of the King

The War of the Ring reaches its climax. At Mount Doom, Frodo fails — Gollum bites off his finger and seizes the Ring, then falls into the Fire (eucatastrophe). Aragorn is crowned King Elessar of Gondor. The hobbits return home to a Shire scouted by Saruman's men (the Scouring). Frodo sails West with the Elves, unable to fully heal from his wounds.

Key characters

  • Frodo — Ring-bearer; the wounded, ordinary hero.
  • Samwise Gamgee — "the truest hero" — loyalty, endurance, love of home.
  • Gandalf — wizard, guide; refuses the Ring (power corrupts).
  • Aragorn — the rightful king who must prove himself.
  • Gollum — Sméagol corrupted by the Ring; Frodo's dark mirror. Pity for Gollum is what saves the world.
  • Sauron — the Dark Lord; pure will to power. Never seen — his absence makes him more terrifying.
"One Ring to rule them all, One Ring to find them, One Ring to bring them all and in the darkness bind them."
— J.R.R. Tolkien, The Lord of the Rings
Work 03

The Silmarillion (1977, posthumous)

Tolkien's mythology

Published posthumously by his son Christopher Tolkien, The Silmarillion contains the deep myths of Middle-earth: the creation of the world by Ilúvatar (God) and the Ainur (angelic powers), the fall of Morgoth (Sauron's master), and the Wars of the First Age. It is Tolkien's Bible and Eddas combined — his attempt to create a mythology for England.

Key essay: On Fairy-Stories (1947)

Tolkien's most important theoretical text. Published in Essays Presented to Charles Williams (1947), it defines the fairy story, defends fantasy as a serious literary form, and introduces the concepts of Sub-creation and Eucatastrophe. Essential for understanding the philosophical underpinning of all his fiction.

"Not all those who wander are lost." — J.R.R. Tolkien, The Fellowship of the Ring
