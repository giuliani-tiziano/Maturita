Themes & Style · J.R.R. Tolkien

Themes & Style

Sub-creation, eucatastrophe, power and the ordinary hero

Theme 01

Sub-creation and the creative act

What is sub-creation?

In On Fairy-Stories (1947), Tolkien argues that the human creative impulse is not mere escapism or imitation — it is a reflection of divine creativity. Man, made in God's image, is a sub-creator: he builds "Secondary Worlds" (fictional realities) that have their own internal logic and beauty.

The test of a successful secondary world is Secondary Belief: while inside it, the reader believes in it fully. This requires internal consistency — which is why Tolkien invented complete languages, genealogies, and calendars for Middle-earth before writing his stories.

"Fantasy is a natural human activity. It certainly does not destroy or even insult Reason; and it does not either blunt the appetite for, nor obscure the perception of, scientific verity."
— J.R.R. Tolkien, On Fairy-Stories (1947)
Theme 02

Eucatastrophe — the joyful turn

Tolkien invented the term eucatastrophe (Greek: eu = good + catastrophe): the sudden, unexpected joyful turn at the end of a fairy story that does not deny the existence of sorrow and failure but overcomes them.

The supreme example in The Lord of the Rings: at Mount Doom, Frodo fails to destroy the Ring — he claims it for himself. It is Gollum who, seizing it in frenzy, falls into the Fire and destroys it. The world is saved not by heroic will but by pity, providence and an "accidental" act.

Tolkien explicitly connected the eucatastrophe to the Resurrection as the ultimate eucatastrophe — the greatest eucatastrophe of history.

Eucatastrophe vs Catastrophe

Theme 03

Power and corruption — the One Ring

The Ring as absolute power

The One Ring is the central symbol of the work. Its verse captures everything: it rules all other rings, finding and binding those who bear them. Even the wisest and most powerful characters — Gandalf, Galadriel — refuse to take it, knowing that its possession would corrupt even their good intentions into tyranny.

Gollum is the Ring's longest victim — a hobbit-like creature reduced over 500 years to a twisted, obsessive shadow of himself ("My precious"). He is Frodo's dark mirror: what Frodo risks becoming.

Industry vs Nature

Mordor and Saruman's Isengard represent industrial devastation: volcanic fires, machinery, deforestation, enslaved workers. The Shire represents rural England — green, communal, pre-industrial. Tolkien hated industrialisation, shaped by WWI devastation and his own nostalgia for the Warwickshire countryside of his childhood.

The Scouring of the Shire (the final chapter before the Epilogue, often cut in adaptations) shows that even the safe haven cannot be spared — Saruman's industrialism has reached home.

Theme 04

The ordinary hero — Frodo and Sam

The "little people" save the world

Unlike traditional epic heroes (Achilles, Beowulf, Aragorn), Frodo and Sam are neither warriors nor kings. They succeed through friendship, loyalty, endurance and pity — not strength or power. Tolkien explicitly intended this: the greatest deeds are accomplished by those who seem least capable.

Sam Gamgee — gardener, servant, devoted friend — is often identified as the "true" hero of The Lord of the Rings. At the end, it is Sam who carries Frodo up the slopes of Mount Doom.

"I can't carry it for you, but I can carry you and it as well. So up you get!"
— Samwise Gamgee, The Return of the King
Theme 05

Death, immortality and grief

The Gift of Men

In Tolkien's mythology, Men are given the "Gift of Ilúvatar" — mortality, the freedom to leave the circles of the world at death. Elves are immortal but bound to the world; they cannot truly die. This means loss is inscribed into the world — the fading of the Elves, the passing of Middle-earth into a new age, the departure of Frodo to the West.

The ending of The Lord of the Rings is deliberately bittersweet: the quest is won, the age of Men begins — but Frodo cannot stay. Some wounds cannot heal in this world. The novel's emotional power comes from Tolkien's refusal of simple happy endings.

Style: language and register

  • Epic register: formal, archaic language for the great moments — modelled on Old English and Norse sagas.
  • Plain speech: Sam and the hobbits speak in colloquial, down-to-earth English — creating contrast and grounding the epic.
  • Invented languages: Quenya and Sindarin (Elvish) are fully grammatical languages used in poems, names and inscriptions within the text.
  • Depth: Tolkien creates the impression of a vast world existing beyond the narrative — footnotes, appendices, untranslated poems — the "iceberg" effect.
