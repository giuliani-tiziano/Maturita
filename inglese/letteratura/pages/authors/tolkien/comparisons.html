Comparison 01

Tolkien vs George Orwell — totalitarianism in myth and realism

Common ground

Both Tolkien and Orwell were deeply concerned with totalitarianism and the abuse of power, writing in the shadow of WWII, Stalin and Hitler. Both created unforgettable images of absolute tyranny: Sauron's all-seeing Eye and Big Brother's telescreens are both symbols of total surveillance and will-to-control.

Both show that power corrupts even those who use it for good intentions — the Rings corrupt even noble characters (Boromir), just as the pigs in Animal Farm become indistinguishable from humans.

Key difference: myth vs political realism

Tolkien uses fantasy and myth — his evil is cosmic and ontological (Sauron is a fallen angelic being who chose to serve Morgoth). His response is moral and spiritual: pity, fellowship, eucatastrophe.

Orwell uses political realism — his evil is bureaucratic, institutional, made of ordinary people following orders. His tone is pessimistic, ending in Winston's total defeat. There is no eucatastrophe in 1984.

Contrast: Tolkien's Ring is destroyed; Orwell's Party endures. Tolkien offers hope; Orwell offers warning.

Comparison 02

Tolkien vs Mary Shelley — forbidden creation and its consequences

Sub-creation vs overreach

Both Tolkien and Shelley engage with the myth of creation — what happens when humans (or beings) try to play God. But their answers differ fundamentally.

Shelley's Frankenstein: Victor creates life from dead matter and abandons his creation. The Creature, denied love and belonging, becomes destructive. Science without responsibility = catastrophe.

Tolkien's Sauron: Sauron creates the Ring to control others' free will — a perversion of sub-creation. Where Tolkien's sub-creator enriches the world while remaining subordinate to God, Sauron seeks to replace all other wills with his own.

The Creature vs Gollum

Both Shelley's Creature and Tolkien's Gollum are figures of tragic pity — created/corrupted by forces beyond their control, capable of both empathy and violence, ultimately destroyed. Both are mirrors of their "creators" (Victor / the Ring).

Key difference: Shelley is a secular rationalist — her "monster" is a product of science and social rejection. Tolkien is a Catholic — his "monster" (Gollum) is a soul corrupted by sin, still capable of grace (he was created by Ilúvatar and his act completes the providential plan).

Comparison 03

Tolkien and the WWI Poets — war and loss

The Somme and the Dead Marshes

Tolkien fought in the Battle of the Somme (1916). The Dead Marshes in The Two Towers — swamps full of the faces of the dead, visible just below the surface — are explicitly connected to his experience of the flooded shell-craters of no man's land, filled with the bodies of soldiers.

But where WWI poets like Wilfred Owen ("Dulce et Decorum Est") write in direct, anti-heroic realism — the ugly truth of gas, mud, death — Tolkien uses myth to transmute the same experience into meaning. The fellowship, the sacrifice, the importance of the "little soldier" — Owen shows war's horror; Tolkien finds, without denying it, the possibility of meaning within it.

"My Sam Gamgee is indeed a reflection of the English soldier, of the privates and batmen I knew in the 1914 war, and recognised as so far superior to myself."
— J.R.R. Tolkien, letter (1956)
