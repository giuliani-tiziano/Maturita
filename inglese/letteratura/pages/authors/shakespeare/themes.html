Themes & Style

Tragedy · Ambition · The Elizabethan World Picture · Blank verse

Theme 01

Tragic structure — Aristotle and hamartia

Shakespeare's tragedies follow the Aristotelian model: a high-born protagonist with a fatal flaw (hamartia) makes a mistake that leads to catastrophe. The audience experiences catharsis — an emotional purging of pity and fear. The structure is five-act: exposition, rising action, climax (Act 3), falling action, catastrophe.

  • Hamlet: hamartia = excessive reflection / paralysis
  • Macbeth: hamartia = unchecked ambition
  • Othello: hamartia = jealousy / credulity
  • Lear: hamartia = pride / failure of judgement

The Elizabethan World Picture

Elizabethans believed in a "Great Chain of Being" — a hierarchical order from God to angels to monarchs to ordinary humans to animals. Disrupting this chain (killing a king, usurping a throne) was a cosmic crime that brought chaos: storms, disease, unnatural events. In Macbeth, Duncan's murder is followed by horses eating each other; in Hamlet, "Denmark is a prison." Order must be restored — usually through the death of the transgressor.

Theme 02

Ambition and power — the Macbeth model

Power corrupts

Macbeth begins as a honourable, celebrated soldier. The prophecy (he will be king) reveals a latent ambition he could have suppressed. Lady Macbeth, more ruthless, provides the final push. But once he kills, each subsequent murder comes more easily: "I am in blood / Stepp'd in so far that, should I wade no more, / Returning were as tedious as go o'er." The tragedy shows how moral corruption is cumulative and progressive.

The role of the supernatural

The witches in Macbeth do not create Macbeth's ambition — they reveal and accelerate it. The question of whether they represent external evil forces or psychological projections of Macbeth's own desires is deliberately left open. Shakespeare is sophisticated about the relationship between prophecy and free will: Macbeth might have become king without murdering anyone.

Style

Shakespeare's dramatic language

  • Blank verse — unrhymed iambic pentameter: the "natural" rhythm of English speech elevated to poetry. Noble characters speak in blank verse; lower characters often in prose
  • Soliloquy — the character alone on stage, thinking aloud: the convention allows direct access to inner psychology
  • Imagery — networks of recurring images in each play (light/dark in Macbeth; disease in Hamlet) create thematic coherence
  • Dramatic irony — the audience knows what a character does not; creates tension and pathos

Iambic pentameter — the basic unit

An iamb is an unstressed syllable followed by a stressed syllable (da-DUM). Pentameter = 5 iambs per line = 10 syllables. "To BE or NOT to BE that IS the QUES-tion" — five iambs, though Shakespeare constantly varies and breaks the metre for dramatic effect. A broken line signals emotional stress; a completed pentameter signals control and authority.

"What a piece of work is a man! How noble in reason, how infinite in faculty!" — Hamlet, Act 2 Scene 2
