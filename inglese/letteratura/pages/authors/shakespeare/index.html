William Shakespeare

Stratford-upon-Avon, 1564 · Stratford-upon-Avon, 1616 — The greatest writer in the English language

Section 01

Life — the essentials

1564
Born — Stratford-upon-Avon
Born in Stratford-upon-Avon, Warwickshire, in the reign of Queen Elizabeth I. His father John was a glove-maker and civic official; his mother Mary Arden came from a prosperous farming family. He attended the King's New School in Stratford, where he would have studied Latin, rhetoric and classical literature.
1582–1585
Marriage and "the lost years"
Married Anne Hathaway at 18 (she was 26). They had three children: Susanna and twins Hamnet and Judith. Between 1585 and 1592, the historical record is blank — the "lost years." Various theories: he may have been a schoolteacher, a soldier, or a travelling player.
1592–1594
London — playwright and actor
By 1592 he was established in London as an actor and playwright, provoking the jealousy of established writers. When plague closed the theatres (1593–94), he turned to poetry, writing the narrative poems Venus and Adonis and The Rape of Lucrece for his patron the Earl of Southampton.
1594
The Lord Chamberlain's Men
Became a founding member and part-owner of the Lord Chamberlain's Men, London's leading acting company. Performed at the Globe Theatre from 1599. Under James I (from 1603) became the King's Men, with royal patronage. Shakespeare's financial success was unusual for a playwright.
1599–1608
The great tragedies
Wrote Julius Caesar (1599), Hamlet (1600–01), Othello (1603), King Lear (1605), Macbeth (1606). Also the Sonnets (published 1609). These works represent the peak of his achievement and some of the greatest writing in any language.
1613
Retirement to Stratford
Retired to Stratford c.1613 having written approximately 37 plays and 154 sonnets. The Globe Theatre burned down in 1613 during a performance of Henry VIII. Died in Stratford in 1616, aged 52.

Key vocabulary

Blank verse
Unrhymed iambic pentameter — Shakespeare's primary dramatic medium; it can sound like natural speech while maintaining formal regularity.
The tragic flaw (hamartia)
From Aristotle: the fatal weakness in a tragic hero — Hamlet's indecision, Macbeth's ambition, Othello's jealousy.
The Globe Theatre
Open-air playhouse built in 1599 in Southwark, London. Capacity ~3,000; no lighting or scenery — language did all the work.
The Elizabethan World Picture
The Renaissance belief that the universe has a natural hierarchy — God, angels, king, nobles, people. Breaking this "Great Chain of Being" causes chaos — the central mechanism of Shakespeare's tragedies.
