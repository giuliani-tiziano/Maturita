Comparisons

Shakespeare vs Beckett · Shakespeare vs Golding · Shakespeare vs Eliot

Comparison 01

Shakespeare vs Beckett — Paralysis on stage

Hamlet — paralysis with cause

Hamlet cannot act because he is caught between reason and passion, between doubt and certainty, between the call of duty and the fear of error. His paralysis is psychologically motivated — he is an intellectual unable to simplify a complex moral situation. The play gives him a ghost, a court, a love interest, a mother — a world of motives and obstacles.

Godot — paralysis without cause

Vladimir and Estragon wait for Godot who will never come. They cannot act because there is nothing to act for or against — no clear moral order has been violated, no ghost commands them, no revenge is required. In Beckett, Hamlet's existential paralysis has become the entire condition of existence, stripped of dramatic machinery. "Nothing to be done" is the first line of the play.

Comparison 02

Shakespeare vs Golding — Human evil

Shakespeare — evil is contextual

Macbeth is not inherently evil — he is a good man corrupted by ambition and circumstance (the witches, Lady Macbeth, his own imagination). Remove the prophecy and the opportunity and Macbeth remains a loyal general. Shakespeare's tragic vision is contextual: given the right conditions, anyone can fall. But the moral order is ultimately restored.

Golding — evil is innate

The boys in Lord of the Flies have no witches, no ambitious spouse, no supernatural prophecy — only a beach, pigs and each other. They choose violence. Golding's moral is that Shakespeare's contextual explanation is too comfortable: evil doesn't need a cause, it needs only the removal of civilisation's thin veneer. The beast is always already there.

Comparison 03

Shakespeare vs T.S. Eliot — Tradition and the modern

Shakespeare as source and standard

T.S. Eliot's critical essay "Hamlet and His Problems" (1919) introduced the concept of the "objective correlative" partly to criticise Hamlet. For Eliot, Hamlet fails because Hamlet's emotion exceeds what the play's situation objectively warrants — his disgust with his mother is too large. Eliot thus used Shakespeare to define his own modernist aesthetic.

The Waste Land and Shakespearean echo

Eliot's The Waste Land (1922) quotes Shakespeare directly: "Those are pearls that were his eyes" (from The Tempest) becomes a haunting refrain in "The Fire Sermon." Shakespeare's language is used as a fragment of a lost cultural wholeness — the greatness of the past juxtaposed with the emptiness of the modern city. Eliot uses Shakespeare the way Macbeth uses Duncan's ghost.

"To be, or not to be, that is the question." — Hamlet, Act 3 Scene 1
