Comparisons

Wilde vs Dickens · Wilde vs Shelley · Wilde vs Orwell

Comparison 01

Wilde vs Dickens — Two visions of Victorian society

Dickens — reform through pathos

Dickens attacks Victorian society through emotional identification with the poor: Oliver Twist, Little Nell, David Copperfield. He wants the reader to feel the injustice and demand reform. His message is explicitly moral and social.

Wilde — critique through irony and paradox

Wilde attacks the same Victorian society but through laughter and wit. Lady Bracknell's absurdity reveals class hypocrisy more efficiently than Dickens's sentimentality. Wilde refuses moral preaching — he lets the comedy expose the emptiness of Victorian values indirectly.

Comparison 02

Wilde vs Mary Shelley — The creator and the created

Frankenstein — the monster as rejected creation

Frankenstein creates beauty (a perfect being from the best human parts) but what results is monstrous. The horror is in the creator's rejection of his creation — which produces the real monster. The lesson: creation requires responsibility and love.

Dorian Gray — the portrait as moral record

Basil creates a portrait of perfect beauty. Dorian's wish separates the beautiful surface from the moral reality — the portrait becomes the "monster" that records Dorian's crimes. Both novels explore the dangerous relationship between creator and creation, beauty and horror.

Comparison 03

Wilde vs Orwell — Art and politics

Wilde's position

"Art for Art's Sake" — art has no moral or political duty. "There is no such thing as a moral or an immoral book. Books are well written, or badly written." Art's only obligation is to beauty.

Orwell's position

"Every line of serious work that I have written since 1936 has been written, directly or indirectly, against totalitarianism." Art is inherently political — the writer who claims neutrality is lying. Clear, honest writing is a political act.

The debate between Wilde and Orwell is the central debate about art's social responsibility — both positions remain defensible.
