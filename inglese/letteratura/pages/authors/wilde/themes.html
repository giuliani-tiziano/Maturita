Oscar Wilde · Themes & Style

Themes & Style

Art for Art's Sake · The Double · Decadence · Wit

Theme 01

Aestheticism — "Art for Art's Sake"

Wilde's central principle, derived from Walter Pater's Studies in the History of the Renaissance (1873): art exists for beauty alone, independent of moral purpose or social utility. Pater's conclusion — "to burn always with this hard, gem-like flame, to maintain this ecstasy, is success in life" — became Wilde's creed.

In Dorian Gray, Lord Henry embodies this philosophy: "The only way to get rid of a temptation is to yield to it." But the novel also shows the destructive consequences of pure aestheticism without moral conscience.

The Aesthetic Movement in context

Against Victorian utilitarianism (art should be morally improving) and didacticism (literature should teach lessons), the Aesthetes asserted that beauty is an end in itself. Other figures: Aubrey Beardsley (visual art), Algernon Swinburne (poetry), Arthur Symons (criticism). The movement was associated with the Decadents and the journal The Yellow Book.

Theme 02

The Double — surface and hidden reality

Dorian and his portrait

The portrait records Dorian's moral reality while his face maintains its beauty — a perfect image of Victorian hypocrisy. The hidden portrait = the secret self that respectable society conceals. Wilde lived this double life: publicly the brilliant wit, privately the homosexual man in a society where homosexuality was illegal.

The theme of double identity recurs throughout Wilde's work. In The Importance of Being Earnest, both protagonists maintain fictional identities ("Bunburying") — they live double lives as a form of freedom from Victorian social obligations. The play treats this playfully; in Dorian Gray, the same theme is tragic.

Style

Wilde's literary style

  • The epigram — short, paradoxical, perfectly balanced ("I can resist everything except temptation")
  • Paradox — inverting conventional wisdom to reveal hidden truth
  • Irony — the gap between what characters say and what Wilde intends
  • Wit and wordplay — puns, reversals, comic misunderstandings
  • Rich, ornate prose — Dorian Gray's luxurious descriptions of art objects

Wilde's paradoxes — exam examples

  • "To define is to limit."
  • "We are all in the gutter, but some of us are looking at the stars."
  • "The truth is rarely pure and never simple."
  • "Experience is the name everyone gives to their mistakes."
  • "A cynic is a man who knows the price of everything and the value of nothing."
