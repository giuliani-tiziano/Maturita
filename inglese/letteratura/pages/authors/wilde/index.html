Oscar Wilde

Dublin, 1854 · Paris, 1900 — The apostle of Aestheticism

Section 01

Life — the essentials

1854
Born — Dublin, Ireland
Oscar Fingal O'Flahertie Wills Wilde born in Dublin to a distinguished family. His father Sir William Wilde was a celebrated surgeon; his mother Lady Jane Wilde ("Speranza") was an Irish nationalist poet. This literary household shaped him profoundly.
1874–1878
Oxford — Aestheticism & First Classé
Studied Classics at Magdalen College, Oxford. Influenced by Walter Pater and John Ruskin. Developed his philosophy of Art for Art's Sake. Won the Newdigate Prize for poetry. Graduated with a First Class degree — despite spending most of his time posing as a dandy.
1881–1882
Celebrity & lecture tour of America
His debut poetry collection published; he became famous as a wit and aesthete before producing major works. Toured America for a year, lecturing on aestheticism. Declared at US customs: "I have nothing to declare except my genius."
1884
Marriage to Constance Lloyd
Married Constance Lloyd; they had two sons, Cyril and Vyvyan. Wilde's homosexuality was a hidden reality throughout his public life.
1890–1895
The golden years — plays and novel
Published The Picture of Dorian Gray (1890), Lady Windermere's Fan (1892), A Woman of No Importance (1893), An Ideal Husband (1895), The Importance of Being Earnest (1895). The height of his fame.
1895
Trial and imprisonment
The Marquess of Queensberry (father of Lord Alfred Douglas, Wilde's lover) accused Wilde of homosexuality. Wilde sued for libel — a catastrophic mistake. Convicted of "gross indecency" and sentenced to two years' hard labour in Reading Gaol. His health was permanently broken.
1898–1900
Exile and death — Paris
Released in 1897; went into exile in France. Wrote The Ballad of Reading Gaol (1898) and De Profundis (a long letter to Douglas, published posthumously). Died of cerebral meningitis in Paris in 1900, aged 46. His last words: "My wallpaper and I are fighting a duel to the death. One of us has to go."

Key vocabulary

Aestheticism
The movement asserting that art exists for its own sake — beauty is the supreme value; art need not be moral or useful.
The Dandy
A man who cultivates an elaborate and artistic appearance; Wilde embodied the dandy both in life and in his fictional characters.
Decadence
The fin-de-siècle tendency to celebrate excess, artifice and moral transgression as artistic values.
Wit / Epigram
Wilde's signature style: short, paradoxical, beautifully constructed sentences that invert conventional wisdom.
