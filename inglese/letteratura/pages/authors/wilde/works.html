Oscar Wilde · Works

Major Works

From fairy tales to society comedies — beauty over morality

Masterwork I

The Picture of Dorian Gray (1890)

Wilde's only novel. Dorian Gray, a beautiful young man, sits for a portrait by the painter Basil Hallward, who adores him. Under the influence of the cynical Lord Henry Wotton, Dorian wishes that the portrait would age instead of him — and the wish is granted. As Dorian pursues a life of hedonism and moral degradation, the portrait grows monstrous. He murders Basil when Basil confronts him about the portrait. Eventually he stabs the portrait — and is found dead, aged and hideous, while the portrait is restored to its original beauty.

The three characters as allegory

Basil Hallward
The artist who sees pure beauty in Dorian — represents the moral, earnest Victorian
Lord Henry Wotton
The cynical aesthete and wit — Wilde's mouthpiece for paradox and Aestheticism
Dorian Gray
Beauty without morality — the dandy who takes Aestheticism to its logical, destructive extreme

The Preface (1891) — the aesthetic manifesto

Wilde added a preface to the second edition consisting entirely of epigrams defending art against moral judgement:

  • "The artist is the creator of beautiful things."
  • "There is no such thing as a moral or an immoral book. Books are well written, or badly written. That is all."
  • "All art is quite useless."
  • "To reveal art and conceal the artist is art's aim."

These epigrams directly refute the Victorian critics who attacked the novel's "immorality."

Masterwork II

The Importance of Being Earnest (1895)

Plot — a trivial comedy for serious people

Jack Worthing and Algernon Moncrieff both maintain fictional alter egos ("Bunbury" / "Ernest") to escape social obligations. Jack plans to marry Gwendolen Fairfax, who insists she can only love a man named Ernest. Algernon uses "Ernest" to woo Jack's ward Cecily in the country. Both women discover the deception. In the resolution, it emerges that Jack really is named Ernest — he was abandoned as a baby in a handbag at Victoria Station.

The play's plot is deliberately trivial — the comedy exposes the absurdity of upper-class Victorian values through wit and paradox. Lady Bracknell's interrogation of Jack is the play's comic centrepiece: she rejects him when she learns he was found in a handbag ("A handbag?") because he lacks a family background.

"To lose one parent, Mr. Worthing, may be regarded as a misfortune; to lose both looks like carelessness." — Lady Bracknell
