Virginia Woolf · Works

Major Works

Revolutionising the English novel — interior life over exterior event

Masterwork I

Mrs Dalloway (1925)

Set on a single day in June 1923 in London. Clarissa Dalloway, an upper-class woman, is preparing a party for that evening. The novel follows her thoughts through the day as she walks through Westminster, shops for flowers, and receives an old admirer, Peter Walsh, who has returned from India. Interwoven is the story of Septimus Warren Smith, a shell-shocked WWI veteran, and his Italian wife Rezia. Septimus has visions of his dead friend Evans and is treated by the callous Dr Bradshaw. Septimus commits suicide before the party. At the party, Clarissa hears of his death and feels a profound connection to his act of resistance.

The double narrative — Clarissa and Septimus

Clarissa Dalloway
Survivor who has made compromises with society — married Richard Dalloway for security, not passion
Septimus Warren Smith
The authentic self destroyed by war and social conformity — he cannot compromise; he dies
The connection
They never meet, but Septimus's death at the end illuminates Clarissa's life — he has done what she cannot

Time and structure

The novel is structured by the chimes of Big Ben, which mark clock time — objective, public, inevitable. Against this, Woolf places "psychological time": memory surges unpredictably, childhood and present exist simultaneously in the mind. The clocks mark duration; the mind is outside duration.

"She felt very young; at the same time unspeakably aged. She sliced like a knife through everything; at the same time was outside, looking on." — Virginia Woolf, Mrs Dalloway
Masterwork II

To the Lighthouse (1927)

Structure — three parts

Part I: The Window
An afternoon and evening at the Ramsays' summer house in the Hebrides. James (aged 6) wants to visit the lighthouse; his father says the weather won't allow it. Mrs Ramsay consoles him. The painter Lily Briscoe struggles with her canvas.
Part II: Time Passes
Ten years in 10 pages: WWI; Mrs Ramsay dies (in brackets); two children die. The house decays. Time destroys what Part I built.
Part III: The Lighthouse
The family returns; they visit the lighthouse (too late for James's childhood wish); Lily finally completes her painting.

The novel is Woolf's most autobiographical — the Ramsay family is based on her own parents. The lighthouse itself is a symbol that resists fixed meaning: it is both a goal and a disappointment, both beautiful and unreachable.

"The great revelation perhaps never did come. Instead there were little daily miracles, illuminations, matches struck unexpectedly in the dark." — Virginia Woolf, To the Lighthouse
"Examine for a moment an ordinary mind on an ordinary day." — Virginia Woolf, Modern Fiction (1925)
