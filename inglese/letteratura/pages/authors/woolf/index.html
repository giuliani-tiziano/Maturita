Virginia Woolf

London, 1882 · Sussex, 1941 — Pioneer of stream of consciousness

Section 01

Life — the essentials

1882
Born — London
Born Adeline Virginia Stephen into a distinguished intellectual Victorian family. Her father Leslie Stephen was a literary critic and editor of the Dictionary of National Biography. The family's social circle included many of the leading thinkers of the age.
1895–1904
Mental illness and family deaths
Her mother Julia died in 1895 — Virginia's first breakdown. Her stepsister Stella died in 1897. Her brother Thoby died of typhoid in 1906. Her father died in 1904. These repeated losses shaped her lifelong struggle with mental illness (now identified as bipolar disorder).
1904
Bloomsbury Group
After her father's death, Virginia and her siblings moved to Bloomsbury. The "Bloomsbury Group" formed — an informal circle of intellectuals including E.M. Forster, John Maynard Keynes, Lytton Strachey and Roger Fry. Central to British Modernism.
1912
Marriage to Leonard Woolf
Married Leonard Woolf, a writer and political thinker. The marriage was one of profound intellectual partnership. Leonard became her primary caregiver during her breakdowns. Together they founded the Hogarth Press (1917), which published her own work and T.S. Eliot's The Waste Land.
1922–1931
The great novels
Published Jacob's Room (1922), Mrs Dalloway (1925), To the Lighthouse (1927), Orlando (1928), The Waves (1931). These novels transformed English fiction through stream of consciousness narration and experimental structure.
1929
A Room of One's Own
Her groundbreaking feminist essay, based on two lectures at Cambridge women's colleges. Its central argument: "a woman must have money and a room of her own if she is to write fiction." Created the fictional figure of "Judith Shakespeare" to illustrate women's historical exclusion from literature.
1941
Death — River Ouse, Sussex
Fearing another mental breakdown while the Blitz raged, and exhausted after completing Between the Acts, Woolf drowned herself in the River Ouse, filling her coat pockets with stones. She left Leonard a final letter: "I don't think two people could have been happier than we have been."

Key vocabulary

Stream of consciousness
A narrative technique that replicates the continuous, associative flow of a character's thoughts — not organised into sentences or logical sequence.
Interior monologue
Direct presentation of a character's thoughts in first person, without a narrating voice — Woolf's primary innovation.
Epiphany
A sudden moment of insight or revelation, often triggered by a seemingly trivial sensory detail.
Modernism
The early 20th-century movement that rejected Victorian literary conventions, exploring subjectivity, time and consciousness.
