George Orwell · Themes & Style

Themes & Style

Totalitarianism · Language as power · Class · Truth

Theme 01

Totalitarianism and the destruction of freedom

Orwell's central theme is the mechanism by which totalitarian states maintain power. In Nineteen Eighty-Four, the Party does not merely demand compliance — it requires genuine belief. Winston is not simply imprisoned; he must be made to love Big Brother. This goes beyond Nazi or Stalinist dictatorship to explore a more fundamental question: can the human mind be completely colonised?

The Party's methods — surveillance, Newspeak, doublethink, the rewriting of history — are designed to make dissent not just dangerous but literally unthinkable.

The telescreen — permanent surveillance

Every citizen has a telescreen that transmits as well as receives. The Thought Police can observe anyone at any time. But crucially, citizens never know when they are being watched — so they must behave as if always watched. This anticipates Michel Foucault's concept of the Panopticon and modern surveillance culture (CCTV, social media monitoring).

Theme 02

Language as a political weapon

Newspeak — the shrinking language

The Party is producing a final edition of the Newspeak Dictionary in which every year words are eliminated, not added. The goal is to reduce vocabulary until complex thought becomes literally impossible. "Crimethink" replaces "heresy" — but the new word carries no emotional resonance, making concepts easier to police.

Orwell's essay Politics and the English Language (1946) argues that vague, abstract political language is deliberately designed to conceal the true meaning of actions (e.g., "pacification" = bombing villages).

This theme connects directly to Orwell's non-fiction. He believed that clear, honest language is a political act — and that the corruption of language enables the corruption of politics. His own prose style is deliberately plain, concrete and precise: "Good prose is like a windowpane."

"If thought corrupts language, language can also corrupt thought." — George Orwell, Politics and the English Language (1946)
Style

Orwell's literary style

  • Plain, direct prose — no ornament or abstraction; concrete nouns and active verbs
  • Documentary realism — drawn from direct personal experience (Burma, Spain, poverty)
  • Irony and satire — especially in Animal Farm: the form of a children's fable to expose adult political horror
  • Dystopian extrapolation — taking real political trends (surveillance, propaganda) to their logical extreme
  • Allegorical structure — Animal Farm as a precise one-to-one mapping of the Russian Revolution

Orwell's six rules for writing (1946)

  1. Never use a long word where a short one will do
  2. If it is possible to cut a word out, cut it out
  3. Never use the passive where you can use the active
  4. Never use a foreign phrase, scientific word or jargon if you can think of an everyday equivalent
  5. Break any of these rules sooner than say anything outright barbarous
"Good prose is like a windowpane." — George Orwell, Why I Write (1946)
