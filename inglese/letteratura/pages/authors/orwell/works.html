George Orwell · Works

Major Works

From documentary realism to political dystopia — 1933 to 1949

Masterwork I

Animal Farm (1945)

A political allegory · 95 pages · rejected 12 times before publication

Plot & Allegory

The animals of Manor Farm rebel against their drunken farmer Mr Jones, inspired by the old boar Old Major's dream of a world without human oppression. They establish "Animalism" — a utopian collective. Gradually, the pigs (led by Napoleon and Snowball) take control. Napoleon expels Snowball and rules through terror, propaganda (Squealer) and brute force (the dogs). The pigs begin walking on two legs and collaborating with humans. Final line: "The creatures outside looked from pig to man, and from man to pig, and from pig to man again; but already it was impossible to say which was which."

Allegorical key

Old Major
Karl Marx / Lenin — the idealist philosopher of revolution
Napoleon
Stalin — the tyrant who betrays the revolution
Snowball
Trotsky — the idealist expelled by the tyrant
Squealer
Soviet propaganda machine (Pravda)
The Dogs
The KGB / Secret Police
Boxer
The loyal, exploited working class
Mr Jones
Tsar Nicholas II

The Seven Commandments → One rule

Initially: "All animals are equal."
Final corruption: "All animals are equal, but some animals are more equal than others."
This captures the gradual rewriting of revolutionary ideals to serve those in power — Orwell's central insight about totalitarianism.

"Four legs good, two legs bad." — The sheep's slogan in Animal Farm — mindless propaganda at its simplest

Publication history

Rejected by Victor Gollancz, Jonathan Cape and T.S. Eliot at Faber & Faber (who called it "Trotskyite" politics). Published by Secker & Warburg in August 1945 — just as the Cold War began. Immediate bestseller.

Masterwork II

Nineteen Eighty-Four (1949)

The defining dystopian novel of the 20th century

Plot summary

Winston Smith works at the Ministry of Truth in Oceania, rewriting historical records to match the Party's current version of reality. He begins a secret love affair with Julia and joins what he believes is a resistance movement led by O'Brien. He is betrayed, arrested and tortured in the Ministry of Love. In Room 101 — where prisoners face their worst fear — Winston is forced to betray Julia. The novel ends with Winston fully broken: "He loved Big Brother."

Key Orwellian concepts

Big Brother
The all-seeing figurehead of the Party — may not exist as an individual
Thought Police
Force that arrests people for "thoughtcrime" — even silent dissent
Doublethink
Holding two contradictory beliefs simultaneously — "War is Peace"
Newspeak
Simplified language designed to make thoughtcrime impossible
Memory hole
Chute for destroying inconvenient historical records
Room 101
Torture room where prisoners face their worst personal fear

The three slogans of the Party

WAR IS PEACE
FREEDOM IS SLAVERY
IGNORANCE IS STRENGTH

These impossible contradictions are central to doublethink — the Party forces citizens to believe all three simultaneously.

The Ministries (ironic names)

  • Ministry of Truth — produces lies and propaganda
  • Ministry of Peace — conducts perpetual war
  • Ministry of Love — tortures and re-educates
  • Ministry of Plenty — manages scarcity
"Who controls the past controls the future. Who controls the present controls the past." — George Orwell, Nineteen Eighty-Four
