George Orwell

Eric Arthur Blair — India, 1903 · London, 1950

Section 01

Life — the essentials

Burma · Paris · London · Spain · Jura · Nobel legacy

1903
Born Eric Arthur Blair — Motihari, India
Born into a lower-upper-middle-class colonial family. His father was an official in the Opium Department of the Indian Civil Service. The family returned to England in 1904.
1922–1927
Imperial Police — Burma
After Eton, joined the Indian Imperial Police in Burma. The experience of enforcing colonial rule generated profound guilt and anti-imperialist convictions. Described in Burmese Days (1934) and the essay "Shooting an Elephant" (1936).
1928–1933
Paris & London — poverty and dishwashing
Lived in deliberate poverty to understand the working class. Worked as a dishwasher in Paris hotels. Documented in Down and Out in Paris and London (1933) — his first book, published under the pen name "George Orwell".
1936
The Road to Wigan Pier — political awakening
Commissioned by the Left Book Club to report on working-class life in northern England. The book combines documentary reportage with political essays on socialism and class.
1936–1937
Spanish Civil War — POUM militiaman
Went to Spain to fight against Franco. Joined the POUM (anti-Stalinist Marxist militia). Shot through the throat by a fascist sniper. Witnessed the Stalinist suppression of the POUM — key experience behind Animal Farm. Described in Homage to Catalonia (1938).
1945
Animal Farm — the allegory of revolution
Published after 12 rejections (publishers feared offending Stalin, a wartime ally). Immediate international success. The allegory of the Russian Revolution and its corruption by Stalinism.
1948–1949
Jura — writing Nineteen Eighty-Four while dying
Wrote his final masterpiece on the Scottish island of Jura while suffering from tuberculosis. The title is thought to be a reversal of the year 1948. Published June 1949.
1950
Death in London — aged 46
Died of tuberculosis in University College Hospital, London, seven months after the publication of Nineteen Eighty-Four. He was 46 years old.

Key vocabulary

Dystopia
An imagined future society characterised by oppression, surveillance and the destruction of individual freedom — the opposite of utopia.
Totalitarianism
A political system in which the state exercises total control over every aspect of life, tolerating no opposition.
Doublethink
Orwell's term for the ability to hold two contradictory beliefs simultaneously and accept both — a tool of the Party's mental control.
Newspeak
The artificial language of Oceania designed to make independent thought impossible by eliminating dangerous words.

Section 02

Political context

From British imperialism to Cold War totalitarianism

The Soviet Union & Animal Farm

Orwell witnessed the Stalinist betrayal of socialist ideals first-hand in Spain (1936–37). Animal Farm (1945) maps the Russian Revolution precisely: Old Major = Marx/Lenin; Napoleon = Stalin; Snowball = Trotsky; the pigs' slogan "All animals are equal, but some animals are more equal than others" = the hypocrisy of Soviet communism.

Nineteen Eighty-Four & Cold War fears

Written as the Cold War began, Nineteen Eighty-Four draws on Nazi Germany, Stalinist Russia and wartime propaganda. Oceania's "Ministry of Truth" (which produces lies), "Room 101" (torture chamber), and the concept of the "unperson" reflect real totalitarian practices Orwell documented as a journalist.

"Big Brother is watching you." — George Orwell, Nineteen Eighty-Four (1949)
