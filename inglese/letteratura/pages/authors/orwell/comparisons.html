Comparisons

Comparison 01

Orwell vs Aldous Huxley — Two dystopias

Nineteen Eighty-Four (Orwell, 1949)

  • Control through fear, pain and terror
  • History is actively rewritten
  • Citizens are kept in poverty and ignorance
  • Language shrunk (Newspeak) to prevent thought
  • Inspired by: Stalinist USSR, Nazi Germany
  • Message: totalitarianism destroys humanity through force

Brave New World (Huxley, 1932)

  • Control through pleasure, soma and conditioning
  • History is simply irrelevant — nobody cares
  • Citizens are kept happy and distracted
  • Deep emotions are pharmacologically suppressed
  • Inspired by: Fordism, mass production, consumer society
  • Message: totalitarianism destroys humanity through pleasure
Neil Postman (1985): "Orwell feared those who would deprive us of information. Huxley feared those who would give us so much information that we would be reduced to passivity and egotism. Orwell feared the truth would be concealed from us. Huxley feared the truth would be drowned in a sea of irrelevance." — Neil Postman, Amusing Ourselves to Death
Comparison 02

Orwell vs William Golding — Human nature

Orwell's view

In Orwell, evil is institutional: the Party, the pigs in power, the state apparatus. Individual humans (Winston, the proles) retain some natural goodness and capacity for love. The system is what corrupts. The solution is political: dismantle totalitarian structures.

Golding's view

In Lord of the Flies, evil is innate: remove the structures of civilisation and children become killers spontaneously. There is no Party forcing Jack to hunt — he chooses it. Golding's pessimism is deeper: the beast is within us, not in the system.

Comparison 03

Orwell vs Mary Shelley — The creator's responsibility

Shelley's Frankenstein (1818)

The scientist creates a being without considering the consequences. The creature becomes monstrous not through nature but through rejection and isolation. The danger is in unchecked scientific ambition and the abandonment of moral responsibility.

Orwell's Party (1949)

The Party has created Newspeak — a language-creature that will eventually make independent thought impossible. Like Frankenstein's monster, it will outlive and possibly destroy its creators. Both works warn against creating systems (scientific or political) without ethical constraints.

"In a time of deceit, telling the truth is a revolutionary act." — Attributed to George Orwell
