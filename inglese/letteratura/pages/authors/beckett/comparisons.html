Comparisons

Comparison 01

Beckett vs Joyce — Language: richness vs reduction

Joyce — maximum density

Joyce's style moves from the relatively simple language of Dubliners to the maximum linguistic complexity of Finnegans Wake — a book written in a language of puns, portmanteau words and multilingual allusions that can be "read" in dozens of ways simultaneously. For Joyce, language is abundance: every word contains more meaning than one reading can exhaust. The world is too full of meaning.

Beckett — maximum reduction

Beckett, who worked as Joyce's secretary and knew Finnegans Wake intimately, went in the opposite direction. He stripped language until almost nothing remained. "Nothing to be done." "I can't go on. I'll go on." The power comes from what is removed, not what is added. Both Irish, both exiled in Paris, writing in French — but diametrically opposite aesthetics. Beckett said: after Joyce there was nothing to add; his only option was to subtract.

Comparison 02

Beckett vs Orwell — Totalitarianism and silence

Orwell — language destroyed by power

In 1984, Newspeak systematically reduces language to make thought impossible — "ungood" instead of "bad"; "crimethink" for any disallowed thought. Language is a political weapon; those in power destroy meaning to maintain control. The reduction of language is caused by an identifiable external agency: the Party.

Beckett — language fails on its own

In Beckett, language does not need a Party to destroy it — it destroys itself. Words cannot communicate what needs to be said; repetition empties them of meaning; silences say more than speech. There is no Ministry of Truth here — only the human inability to mean what we say or say what we mean. The failure of language is ontological (a condition of being), not political.

Comparison 03

Beckett vs Eliot — The response to desolation

Eliot — desolation overcome by faith

The Waste Land (1922) describes spiritual desolation after WWI. Its famous final word is "Shantih" — the Sanskrit peace that passes understanding. Eliot converted to Anglo-Catholicism in 1927; his later work (Four Quartets, 1943) finds "the still point of the turning world" — a spiritual resolution. The waste land was a crisis, not a permanent condition.

Beckett — desolation as permanent condition

Beckett saw in Eliot's religious resolution an escape that post-WWII reality could not support. After Auschwitz, after the atomic bomb, "Shantih" is not available. "I can't go on. I'll go on." — there is no resolution, only the stubborn continuation of existence without meaning or consolation. Beckett represents the terminus of the Modernist project: having dismantled all certainties, there is nothing to replace them with. You go on anyway.

"I can't go on. I'll go on." — Samuel Beckett, The Unnamable
