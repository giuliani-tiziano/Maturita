Text 01

Waiting for Godot — Opening exchange (Act I)

A country road. A tree. Evening.

Estragon, sitting on a low mound, is trying to take off his boot. He pulls at it with both hands, panting. He gives up, exhausted, rests, tries again.

ESTRAGON: Nothing to be done.

VLADIMIR: I'm beginning to come round to that opinion. All my life I've tried to put it from me, saying, Vladimir, be reasonable, you haven't yet tried everything. And I resumed the struggle.

So there you are again.

ESTRAGON: Am I?

VLADIMIR: I'm glad to see you back. I thought you'd gone forever.

ESTRAGON: Me too.

"Nothing to be done" — the first line

The play opens on Estragon struggling with a boot — a piece of vaudeville comedy. His first line, "Nothing to be done," refers to the boot. But Vladimir immediately elevates it to a philosophical statement about human existence: "I've tried to put it from me... I resumed the struggle." The trivial (a boot) and the profound (the human condition) are inseparable in Beckett. Every small difficulty is a figure of the larger impossibility.

Minimalism and the stage direction

"A country road. A tree. Evening." — Beckett's stage direction strips setting to three elements. This is not vagueness but precision: the road suggests movement (possible) but also futility (no destination); the tree suggests life (barely) and also the possibility of hanging (they discuss it). "Evening" — day is ending; darkness is coming. The setting contains the whole play in three nouns.

Text 02

Waiting for Godot — The circular ending (Act I and Act II)

Act I ending:

VLADIMIR: Well, shall we go?
ESTRAGON: Yes, let's go.
They do not move.
CURTAIN


Act II ending:

ESTRAGON: Well, shall we go?
VLADIMIR: Yes, let's go.
They do not move.
CURTAIN

The reversed exchange

The ending of Act I and Act II are structurally identical — except that in Act II, Estragon asks the question that Vladimir asked in Act I, and vice versa. This tiny reversal makes the repetition explicit: the days are interchangeable. Who speaks first does not matter. The action ("they do not move") is the same. The structure of the play argues that tomorrow will be the same as today — and yesterday.

Action and language divorced

The most famous stage direction in modern theatre: "They do not move." The characters say they will go — but they cannot move. Language and action are completely divorced. This is the theatre of the absurd in its purest form: speech performs one thing (decision, intention, will) while the stage shows another (paralysis, stasis, impossibility). The gap between word and act is the human condition.

"Let's go." "Yes, let's go." They do not move. — Samuel Beckett, Waiting for Godot
