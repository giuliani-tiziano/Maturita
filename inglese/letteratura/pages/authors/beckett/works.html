Samuel Beckett · Works

Major Works

Waiting for Godot · Endgame — Theatre of the Absurd

Masterwork I

Waiting for Godot (En attendant Godot, Paris 1953 / London 1955)

"A tragicomedy in two acts." Two tramps — Vladimir (Didi) and Estragon (Gogo) — wait on a country road near a bare tree for someone called Godot. He does not come. A boy arrives each act to say that Godot will come tomorrow. In each act, Pozzo (a self-important master) and Lucky (his enslaved porter, who wears a rope around his neck) pass by. In Act II the tree has sprouted four or five leaves; Pozzo is now blind; Lucky, dumb. At the end of each act: "Well, shall we go?" "Yes, let's go." They do not move. The curtain falls.

Key elements of the play

Godot
Never identified — God? A saviour? An employer? Purpose itself? The point is the not-knowing
The bare tree
Desolation; the only change between acts is its four leaves — minimal, ambiguous hope
Circular structure
Act II almost exactly repeats Act I — the days are all the same; waiting is permanent
Vaudeville comedy
Pratfalls, misunderstandings, boot-pulling — the absurdity of the human condition made comic
Masterwork II

Endgame (Fin de partie, Paris 1957)

The last shelter

A single room, two high windows, four characters: Hamm (blind, in a wheelchair, cannot stand), Clov (cannot sit, constantly threatening to leave but never leaving), Nagg and Nell (Hamm's parents, legless, in dustbins). Outside: nothing. The world has apparently ended. The play is slower, darker and more formally austere than Godot. The title: the endgame in chess, when few pieces remain and each move brings the game closer to its inevitable conclusion.

"CLOV: Why do you keep me?
HAMM: There's no one else.
CLOV: There's nowhere else." — Samuel Beckett, Endgame
"Nothing to be done." — Vladimir, Waiting for Godot, opening line
