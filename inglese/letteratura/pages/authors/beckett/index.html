Samuel Beckett

Dublin, 1906 · Paris, 1989 — Waiting for meaning

Section 01

Life — the essentials

1906
Born — Foxrock, Dublin
Born Samuel Barclay Beckett in Foxrock, a prosperous suburb of Dublin, on Good Friday (an irony he appreciated). His family were Protestant Irish. He received an excellent education at Portora Royal School (also attended by Oscar Wilde) and Trinity College Dublin.
1927–1930
Paris — friendship with James Joyce
Went to Paris as a lecteur d'anglais at the École Normale Supérieure. Met James Joyce and became his assistant and friend — helping him read, transcribing dictation for Finnegans Wake. Joyce's radical formal experiments profoundly influenced Beckett's own direction.
1937
Settled in Paris permanently
After years of moving between Dublin, London and Paris, settled in Paris. Was stabbed by a pimp in the street in 1938 — while visiting him in hospital, he met Suzanne Deschevaux-Dumesnil, who became his lifelong companion and wife (married 1961).
1940–1945
French Resistance
After the fall of France, joined the French Resistance, working as a courier for the Gloria SMH network. The network was betrayed in 1942; Beckett and Suzanne fled to the unoccupied zone, hiding in the village of Roussillon for the rest of the war. He later said: "I preferred France at war to Ireland at peace."
1945–1950
The "siege in the room" — revelation and major works
After the war, returned to Dublin to see his sick mother. Had a "revelation" in his mother's room: he understood that his art must embrace darkness and failure rather than seeking to transcend them. Wrote his trilogy (Molloy, Malone Dies, The Unnamable) and Waiting for Godot in this period — all in French first.
1953
Waiting for Godot — premiere in Paris
Premiered at the Théâtre de Babylone, Paris, on 5 January 1953. An immediate sensation: described as a play in which nothing happens, twice. Initially baffling, then recognised as a masterpiece. The English premiere in London (1955) was controversial; but by the 1960s it had transformed world theatre.
1969
Nobel Prize in Literature
Awarded "for his writing, which — in new forms for the novel and drama — in the destitution of modern man acquires its elevation." Beckett was in Tunisia; Suzanne accepted on his behalf. He called it a "catastrophe" (also the title of one of his plays).

Key vocabulary

Theatre of the Absurd
Term coined by critic Martin Esslin for plays that dramatise the meaninglessness of human existence — no coherent plot, no resolution, characters in circular, futile situations.
Waiting
Beckett's central image: characters wait for something (Godot, death, meaning) that never arrives — making waiting itself the subject, not what is waited for.
Nothing
"Nothing to be done" — the opening line of Waiting for Godot — encapsulates Beckett's philosophical position: a radical nihilism that refuses to sentimentalise emptiness.
Minimalism
Beckett's style progressively stripped away: Happy Days buries the character to her neck; Breath lasts 35 seconds with no characters at all.
