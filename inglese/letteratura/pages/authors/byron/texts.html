Annotated poems for the Maturità oral exam

Text 01

"She Walks in Beauty" (1815)

She walks in beauty, like the night
Of cloudless climes and starry skies;
And all that's best of dark and bright
Meet in her aspect and her eyes;
Thus mellowed to that tender light
Which heaven to gaudy day denies.

One shade the more, one ray the less,
Had half impaired the nameless grace
Which waves in every raven tress,
Or softly lightens o'er her face;
Where thoughts serenely sweet express,
How pure, how dear their dwelling-place.

And on that cheek, and o'er that brow,
So soft, so calm, yet eloquent,
The smiles that win, the tints that glow,
But tell of days in goodness spent,
A mind at peace with all below,
A heart whose love is innocent!

The beauty of contrasts

Written after Byron saw his cousin Mrs Robert John Wilmot at a ball, wearing a black dress with spangles. The central image is paradoxical: her beauty is the meeting of dark and light — "cloudless climes and starry skies" — a night sky full of stars. This makes her beauty more complex and less conventional than the standard "sunny" beauty of other poets.

"All that's best of dark and bright / Meet in her aspect" — her beauty is not simply light (which is "gaudy") but a perfect balance.

Outer beauty = inner virtue

The final stanza breaks from pure description to moral assertion: her beauty is evidence of "days in goodness spent," "a heart whose love is innocent." Byron's poem is ultimately conventional in its Romantic linking of outer beauty and inner virtue — but the beauty he celebrates is unusual: nocturnal, mysterious, dark-and-bright. The Byronic sensibility prefers night to "gaudy day."

Text 02

Childe Harold's Pilgrimage — Canto IV (Rome, 1818)

There is a pleasure in the pathless woods,
There is a rapture on the lonely shore,
There is society, where none intrudes,
By the deep Sea, and music in its roar:
I love not Man the less, but Nature more,
From these our interviews, in which I steal
From all I may be, or have been before,
To mingle with the Universe, and feel
What I can ne'er express, yet cannot all conceal.

Byron and nature — different from Wordsworth

Byron loves nature — but for different reasons than Wordsworth. Where Wordsworth finds in nature a moral teacher and a source of childhood memory, Byron finds in it escape from society and the self ("From all I may be, or have been before"). Nature is not a teacher but a refuge — for the exile who does not fit in human society.

"Pathless woods," "lonely shore" — these are the landscapes of the outsider, not the Lake District of the domesticated Romantic.

The sublime and the inexpressible

"What I can ne'er express, yet cannot all conceal" — the Romantic sublime is an experience that language cannot fully capture. Byron finds in the ocean's "music" something beyond articulation. This sense of the inexpressible — the sublime as a limit of language — is shared with Wordsworth's "Something far more deeply interfused" in Tintern Abbey.

"There is a pleasure in the pathless woods, / There is a rapture on the lonely shore." — Lord Byron, Childe Harold's Pilgrimage
