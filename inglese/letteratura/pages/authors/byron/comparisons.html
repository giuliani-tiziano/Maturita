Comparisons

Byron vs Wordsworth · Byron vs Wilde · Byron vs Shelley (Mary)

Comparison 01

Byron vs Wordsworth — Two Romanticisms

Byron — rebel, exile, political

Byron's Romanticism is about passion, rebellion, political engagement and self-destruction. He remains radical throughout his life, attacks the establishment consistently, and dies for a political cause. Nature is refuge from society, not a moral teacher. He is the Romantic as celebrity and scandalist.

Wordsworth — reconciliation, contemplation, conservative

Wordsworth's Romanticism is about memory, nature, and the growth of consciousness. He began as a radical but became conservative. He found comfort in the natural world and ultimately in conventional religion. He is the Romantic as sage — revered in old age; Byron is the Romantic as shooting star — brilliant, brief, destructive.

Comparison 02

Byron vs Oscar Wilde — The outsider celebrity

Byron as celebrity

Byron was the first poet to become a celebrity in the modern sense: mobbed by fans, pursued by women, his portrait hung in drawing rooms across Europe. He cultivated his image — the brooding, club-footed lord — and lived it. The persona and the person became inseparable. He used scandal as publicity.

Wilde as celebrity

Wilde also cultivated a public persona (the wit, the dandy, the aesthete) and used it for publicity. Both men were destroyed by their refusal to conform — Byron by exile, Wilde by imprisonment. Both created a literary type (Byronic Hero / Wildean dandy) partly from self-observation. Both are cautionary tales of charisma and self-destruction.

Comparison 03

Byron and Mary Shelley — Villa Diodati (1816)

The connection

Byron, Percy Shelley, Mary Godwin and Polidori spent the "Year Without a Summer" at Villa Diodati on Lake Geneva. From this meeting emerged: Mary's Frankenstein, Polidori's The Vampyre (based on Byron), Byron's "The Darkness" and "The Prisoner of Chillon." The most fertile creative collision in Romantic history.

Two visions of the Promethean creator

Byron's Prometheus (Prometheus, 1816): "Thy Godlike crime was to be kind, / To render with thy precepts less / The sum of human wretchedness." — Prometheus as heroic rebel. Mary's Frankenstein: Prometheus punished for transgression, unable to control his creation. Same myth, opposite moral conclusions — reflecting Byron's optimism and Shelley's anxiety about creation.

"I awoke one morning and found myself famous." — Lord Byron (on the publication of Childe Harold)
