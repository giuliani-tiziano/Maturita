Lord Byron · Themes & Style

Themes & Style

The Byronic Hero · Freedom · Exile · Nature

Theme 01

The Byronic Hero — the defining Romantic type

Byron created a literary type that dominated European Romanticism and continues to resonate today. The Byronic Hero is:

  • Handsome and magnetic but self-destructive
  • Haunted by a mysterious past sin or guilt
  • Defiant of social norms, morality and religion
  • Capable of great love but bringing destruction to those he loves
  • An exile from society — by choice or by banishment
  • Ultimately tragic — condemned by his own nature

Byron himself was the Byronic Hero — his life made no distinction between the literary type and the man. This made him both enormously attractive and deeply dangerous in Victorian eyes.

Descendants of the Byronic Hero

  • Heathcliff (Emily Brontë, Wuthering Heights, 1847)
  • Rochester (Charlotte Brontë, Jane Eyre, 1847)
  • Dorian Gray (Oscar Wilde, 1890) — aesthete version
  • James Bond — the 20th-century popular version
  • The vampire — Polidori's The Vampyre (1819) was directly based on Byron
Theme 02

Political freedom and revolutionary idealism

Byron as political poet

Byron championed oppressed peoples: Greeks fighting Ottoman rule, Italians under Austrian occupation (he joined the Carbonari), Irish Catholics under British rule. He supported the Luddites in the House of Lords (his maiden speech). He died in Greece fighting for independence. His politics were his life, not a literary pose.

This political commitment distinguishes Byron from Wordsworth. Wordsworth began as a radical supporter of the French Revolution but became conservative. Byron remained radical throughout. His satire Don Juan attacks the establishment consistently; his death in Greece was a deliberate political act.

Style

Byron's literary style

  • Spenserian stanza — used in Childe Harold: 9 lines, flowing, allows digression and meditation
  • Ottava rima — used in Don Juan: 8 lines, allows comic deflation of serious subjects
  • Wit and satire — Byron's most personal voice; sharp, ironic, impatient with pretension
  • Speed and energy — "Easy, careless, tender, galloping" — his verse at its best feels improvised, effortless
  • Lyric beauty — "She Walks in Beauty" shows Byron's capacity for pure lyric melody

Byron and Romanticism

Byron disliked being called a Romantic — he admired the 18th-century poet Alexander Pope and attacked Wordsworth and Coleridge as obscure. Yet his work is the most internationally influential expression of Romanticism: the Byronic Hero swept across Europe, inspiring Pushkin, Stendhal, Heine and a generation of revolutionary artists and politicians.

"I have not loved the world, nor the world me." — Lord Byron, Childe Harold's Pilgrimage
