Lord Byron

London, 1788 · Missolonghi, 1824 — The Byronic Hero

Section 01

Life — the essentials

1788
Born — London
George Gordon Byron born in London. His father died when he was three. He inherited the title Lord Byron at age 10 from a great-uncle. He was born with a clubfoot, which caused him lifelong psychological pain — he compensated with physical prowess (he was an excellent swimmer) and extreme behaviour.
1805–1807
Cambridge and first poetry
Studied at Trinity College, Cambridge — more notorious for his bear (which he kept as a pet, since dogs were banned) than his studies. Published Hours of Idleness (1807), which was savagely reviewed. His angry reply, English Bards and Scotch Reviewers (1809), established his poetic voice.
1809–1811
Grand Tour — Greece, Turkey, Albania
Toured the Mediterranean: Portugal, Spain, Malta, Albania, Greece, Turkey. Swam the Hellespont (the strait between Europe and Asia). The journey provided the material for Childe Harold's Pilgrimage — a verse travel narrative that made him famous overnight.
1812
"Woke up famous" — Childe Harold
Cantos I and II of Childe Harold's Pilgrimage published. Byron wrote: "I awoke one morning and found myself famous." He became the most celebrated literary celebrity in England — mobbed, adored, and the object of enormous scandal due to his affairs (including with his half-sister Augusta Leigh).
1816
Self-exile — the Byron scandal
Separated from his wife Lady Byron amid rumours of incest, sodomy and madness. Left England permanently. At the Villa Diodati on Lake Geneva he met Percy and Mary Shelley and wrote "The Prisoner of Chillon" and began Manfred. Settled in Venice, then Ravenna and Genoa.
1824
Death — Missolonghi, Greece
Went to Greece to join the war of independence against the Ottomans (he financed his own brigade). Died of fever in Missolonghi, aged 36. He was mourned as a national hero in Greece. In England he was denied burial in Westminster Abbey (granted to him only in 1969).

Key vocabulary

The Byronic Hero
A brooding, rebellious, passionate anti-hero haunted by a mysterious past; proud, cynical, sexually magnetic. Byron embodied this type in both his life and characters.
Satire
Byron used wit and irony (especially in Don Juan) to mock the hypocrisies of English society.
Ottava rima
An Italian verse form (8 lines, ABABABCC rhyme) used brilliantly by Byron in Don Juan and Beppo — allowing rapid shifts between seriousness and comedy.
Philhellenism
Love of Greece and Greek culture; Byron championed Greek independence, dying for it.
