Lord Byron · Works

Major Works

From Childe Harold to Don Juan — the Byronic hero in verse

Key Work I

Childe Harold's Pilgrimage (1812–1818)

A verse travelogue in Spenserian stanzas (nine-line stanzas, ABABBCBCC rhyme) following the melancholy Harold across Portugal, Spain, Albania, Greece and Belgium. Harold is world-weary, disillusioned, seeking sensation — the first major Byronic Hero. Cantos I and II (1812) made Byron famous overnight. Canto III (1816) reflects his own exile after the scandal. Canto IV (1818) meditates on Rome and historical ruins.

The Byronic Hero — key traits

Brooding melancholy
A mysterious, undefined sorrow — often linked to a dark past
Defiance
Refuses to conform to social norms; scornful of conventional morality
Passionate intensity
Loves deeply but destroys those he loves; cannot be contained
Exile
An outsider by choice or necessity — self-exiled from society
Pride
Never submits; even in defeat maintains tragic dignity

Canto III — after Waterloo

Written after Byron left England in disgrace (1816). The famous stanzas on the eve of Waterloo — "There was a sound of revelry by night" (the Duchess of Richmond's ball) — describe the clash between festivity and carnage. Napoleon is both villain and tragic hero in Byron's vision: "the greatest, nor the worst of men."

"I have not loved the world, nor the world me,
But let us part fair foes; I do believe,
Though I have found them not, that there may be
Words which are things — hopes which will not deceive." — Byron, Childe Harold's Pilgrimage, Canto III
Key Work II

Don Juan (1819–1824, unfinished)

The anti-hero

Byron inverts the Don Juan legend: rather than the seducer, his Don Juan is the seduced — women pursue him. The poem is a comic epic in ottava rima (8-line stanzas, ABABABCC) — a form that allows Byron to move rapidly between sublime and comic, serious and satirical. The poem attacks English society, war, marriage, cant and hypocrisy.

Byron wrote 17 cantos before his death in Greece. Don Juan travels from Spain to Greece, Turkey, Russia and England. In each setting Byron satirises different forms of social hypocrisy — the poem is his most complete critique of his age. The mock-heroic tone allows him to praise ideals (love, liberty, nature) while deflating pretension.

"Man's love is of man's life a thing apart, / 'Tis woman's whole existence." — Byron, Don Juan
"She walks in beauty, like the night / Of cloudless climes and starry skies." — Lord Byron, She Walks in Beauty (1815)
