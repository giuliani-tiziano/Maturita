Charles Dickens

Portsmouth, 1812 · Kent, 1870 — The voice of Victorian England

Section 01

Life — the essentials

1812
Born — Portsmouth
Born in Landport, Portsmouth. His father John was charming but financially irresponsible — a characteristic later satirised in the character of Mr Micawber. The family moved frequently and fell steadily into debt.
1824
Warren's Blacking Factory — the defining trauma
Aged 12, sent to work at Warren's boot-blacking factory while his father was imprisoned in the Marshalsea Debtors' Prison. He worked ten hours a day sticking labels on pots of boot polish. This experience of child labour, humiliation and abandonment never left him and became the emotional core of all his fiction.
1833–1836
Journalist and Sketches by Boz
Worked as a parliamentary reporter and journalist. Published Sketches by Boz (1836), sketches of London life. His ability to capture street characters and social types came directly from his years as a reporter walking London's streets at all hours.
1836–1839
The Pickwick Papers and serial fiction
The Pickwick Papers serialised in monthly instalments — an instant phenomenon. Dickens pioneered the serial novel, writing month by month as readers demanded more. This gave his novels their characteristic loose, episodic structure and their variety of sub-plots.
1843–1850
The great social novels
Published A Christmas Carol (1843), Dombey and Son (1848), David Copperfield (1850 — his own favourite). Oliver Twist (1837) had already attacked the New Poor Law; now he became the pre-eminent voice of social reform in Victorian England.
1852–1861
The masterworks
Published Bleak House (1852), Hard Times (1854), Little Dorrit (1857), A Tale of Two Cities (1859), Great Expectations (1861) — widely considered his finest novel.
1870
Death mid-novel
Died of a stroke at 58, leaving The Mystery of Edwin Drood unfinished. Buried in Poets' Corner, Westminster Abbey — despite his request for a modest funeral. The public lined the streets.

Key vocabulary

Social novel
A novel that exposes and critiques social injustice — poverty, child labour, the legal system, the workhouse — with the aim of reforming public opinion.
Caricature
Dickens's technique of defining characters through exaggerated, repeated physical and verbal mannerisms — Uriah Heep's "umble," Mr Micawber's pompous rhetoric.
Serial publication
Dickens published in monthly or weekly instalments, ending each with a cliffhanger to maintain readers' attention.
The Victorian compromise
The gap between the Victorians' public morality and private reality — a central tension in all Dickens's work.
