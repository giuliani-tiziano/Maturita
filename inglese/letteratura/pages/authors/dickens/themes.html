Charles Dickens · Themes & Style

Themes & Style

Social injustice · Class · Childhood · The Victorian compromise

Theme 01

Social injustice and the Victorian class system

Dickens wrote in the Victorian period (1837–1901) of extraordinary economic growth and equally extraordinary inequality. The Industrial Revolution had created both the middle class and the urban poor — both figures dominate his fiction. He attacked specific institutions: the workhouse (Oliver Twist), the Chancery courts (Bleak House), the debtors' prison (Little Dorrit), the factory system (Hard Times).

Unlike Marx (writing at the same time), Dickens believed that social reform could be achieved through individual moral transformation — the conversion of Scrooge in A Christmas Carol is the paradigm.

The Victorian Compromise

Victorian society maintained a gap between its public morality (respectability, religion, family values) and its private reality (prostitution, child labour, poverty). Dickens's novels constantly expose this gap. Miss Havisham in her rotting wedding dress is the Victorian Compromise made literal: the obsessive maintenance of a fiction of propriety while reality decays beneath.

Theme 02

Childhood and the child's perspective

The child as moral centre

Dickens's child protagonists — Oliver, Pip, David Copperfield, Little Nell — are innocent victims of an adult world that exploits them. The child sees clearly what adults have learned to ignore: the injustice of the workhouse, the cruelty of the factory, the hypocrisy of the class system. Children in Dickens are the measure of a society's moral health.

This reflects Dickens's own childhood trauma (the blacking factory) which he never fully processed and could not speak of publicly. But it also connects to the Romantic tradition (Blake's chimney sweeps, Wordsworth's child as father of the man) and to the Victorian sentimental cult of childhood innocence. Dickens weaponised sentiment — he made his readers cry, and then demanded social change.

Style

Dickens's literary style

  • Caricature — characters defined by obsessive repeated traits (Uriah Heep's humility, Micawber's rhetoric)
  • Serial structure — written in monthly instalments; each ends with suspense
  • Pathetic fallacy — weather and environment mirror emotional states
  • Humour and pathos — comedy and tragedy juxtaposed (the death of Little Nell; Mr Pickwick)
  • Satirical exaggeration — institutions shown at their most absurd and hypocritical

Dickens and the Victorian novel

Dickens's novels are long, episodic, full of coincidence and melodrama — shaped by serialisation. This is often criticised as structurally weak. But it also gave Dickens extraordinary freedom: he could respond to reader reaction, extend characters who became popular, and change direction. His best novels (Bleak House, Great Expectations) show him mastering the form's constraints.
