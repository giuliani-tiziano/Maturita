Comparison 01

Dickens vs Orwell — Two visions of oppression

Dickens — Victorian capitalism

Dickens attacks private exploitation (the factory owner, the moneylender, the corrupt lawyer) and government negligence (the workhouse, the Chancery). His villain is greed and hypocrisy; his remedy is moral transformation — individual conversion, like Scrooge. He believes in the possibility of reform within the existing system.

Orwell — 20th-century totalitarianism

Orwell attacks a system so complete that individual moral transformation is impossible — the Party destroys the ability to conceive of alternatives. Where Dickens's Oliver can be saved by a kind benefactor, Winston Smith has no Mr Brownlow to rescue him. The 20th century made Dickens's optimism impossible.

Comparison 02

Dickens vs Wilde — Class and society

Dickens — moral outrage

Dickens attacks class injustice through pathos and moral outrage — the reader should weep for Oliver and be ashamed of the workhouse system. The method is emotional identification. Dickens wants social reform as a moral imperative.

Wilde — comic exposure

Wilde attacks the same class system through wit and paradox — Lady Bracknell's absurdity exposes the emptiness of Victorian class values more efficiently than Dickens's sentiment. Wilde does not want reform; he wants to show that the whole edifice is ridiculous. Laughter rather than tears.

Comparison 03

Dickens vs Golding — The innocent child

Dickens — innate goodness survives

In Dickens, children are essentially innocent — they may be corrupted by society (Fagin's gang, the workhouse) but their natural goodness survives and reasserts itself. Oliver remains fundamentally good despite everything. The child is the moral standard that society fails to meet.

Golding — innocence is an illusion

In Lord of the Flies, British schoolboys of the same class and background as Dickens's rescued heroes descend into savagery without any adult corruption or factory system to blame. Golding directly counters the Victorian belief in childhood innocence — the beast is within the child from the beginning.
