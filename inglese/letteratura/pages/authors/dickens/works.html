Charles Dickens · Works

Major Works

Great Expectations · Oliver Twist — society through the eyes of a child

Masterwork I

Great Expectations (1861)

Dickens's finest novel — a Bildungsroman narrated in the first person by Philip Pirrip ("Pip"). Pip is a poor blacksmith's apprentice in Kent. He is secretly given money by an unknown benefactor to become a "gentleman." He believes his benefactor is the eccentric Miss Havisham (who stopped all the clocks at the moment her fiancé jilted her at the altar, and has worn her wedding dress and decaying cake ever since). He falls in love with Estella, Miss Havisham's beautiful ward, trained to break men's hearts. His real benefactor is revealed to be the escaped convict Magwitch, whom Pip helped as a child.

Key characters and their symbolic meaning

Pip
The aspiring lower-class child; social mobility as both liberation and corruption
Miss Havisham
Time stopped; the refusal to accept loss; the danger of obsessive grief
Estella
Bred to be heartless; the corruption of emotional capacity by ideology
Magwitch
The convict as true benefactor; social guilt and redemption; Australia as escape from class
Joe Gargery
Loyalty, kindness and dignity without "gentlemanly" status — Dickens's moral standard

Two endings — Dickens and Bulwer-Lytton

Dickens originally wrote a sad ending: Pip and Estella meet years later and part finally. His friend Edward Bulwer-Lytton persuaded him to change it to an ambiguous but more hopeful ending. The original ending is arguably stronger. Both versions are studied.

"Suffering has been stronger than all other teaching... I have been bent and broken, but — I hope — into a better shape." — Estella, Great Expectations

The theme of "gentility"

The novel questions what it means to be a "gentleman." Pip gains money and education but loses his moral integrity — he is ashamed of Joe. By the end he has understood that Joe's natural dignity and loyalty are worth more than all his "great expectations." True gentility is a moral quality, not a social position.

Masterwork II

Oliver Twist (1837)

The attack on the Poor Law

The New Poor Law (1834) created workhouses where the poor were forced to labour in degrading conditions — the theory being that conditions should be bad enough to deter anyone who was not genuinely destitute. Dickens savagely satirises this: Oliver, asking for more food ("Please, sir, I want some more"), is treated as a criminal.

Oliver is a pure-natured orphan who, despite being raised in the underworld of Fagin's gang, maintains his innate goodness. This is Dickens's most optimistic view of human nature — but the novel also shows the reality of juvenile crime and prostitution (Nancy) in a way that shocked Victorian readers.

"It was the best of times, it was the worst of times." — Charles Dickens, A Tale of Two Cities
