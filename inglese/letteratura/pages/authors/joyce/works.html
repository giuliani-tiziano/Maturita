James Joyce · Works

Major Works

From Dublin's paralysis to the revolution of the novel

Masterwork I

Dubliners (1914)

Fifteen short stories about Dublin life, organised in four stages: childhood, adolescence, maturity, and public life. Joyce wrote them in what he called a style of "scrupulous meanness" — plain, spare, unadorned prose that allows the irony to emerge from the situation rather than the narrator's commentary.

The collection's central concept is paralysis: Dublin — and by extension Ireland — is morally, socially and spiritually paralysed. Characters want to escape but cannot. The final story, "The Dead," is Joyce's masterpiece of the collection and one of the finest short stories in any language.

The epiphany in "Araby"

Setup
A boy is infatuated with his friend's sister. He promises to bring her something from Araby — an exotic bazaar
Arrival
He arrives late; most stalls are closing; the one stall open is attended by bored English assistants speaking of trivial things
Epiphany
"Gazing up into the darkness I saw myself as a creature driven and derided by vanity; and my eyes burned with anguish and anger." — The romantic ideal collapses into squalid reality

"The Dead" (last story)

Gabriel Conroy attends his aunts' annual Christmas party, feeling superior to the parochial Dublin world around him. His wife Gretta is moved by a song — Gabriel assumes she is thinking of him. She reveals she is thinking of Michael Furey, a young man who died for love of her. Gabriel has an epiphany: he has never felt such passion; he does not truly know his wife; the dead have a claim on the living that the merely living cannot match.

The famous final paragraph — snow falling across Ireland, over the living and the dead — is among the most celebrated endings in literature.

Masterwork II

Ulysses (1922)

Structure and Homer

Set on 16 June 1904 in Dublin. Eighteen episodes, each corresponding to an episode in Homer's Odyssey. Leopold Bloom (= Odysseus) is a middle-aged Jewish advertising agent. Stephen Dedalus (= Telemachus) is an aspiring artist. Molly Bloom (= Penelope) is a concert singer. The "Lotus-Eaters," "Cyclops," "Sirens," "Circe" — all have Dublin counterparts.

Each episode also has its own literary style, body organ, colour and symbol. The "Circe" episode is written as a hallucinatory play. "Eumaeus" uses deliberately tired, clichéd prose. "Penelope" — Molly Bloom's monologue — is 40 pages without punctuation, the purest stream of consciousness in literature.
