James Joyce · Themes & Style

Themes & Style

Paralysis · Epiphany · Exile · The mythic method

Theme 01

Paralysis — the diagnosis of Dublin

Joyce wrote to his publisher that Dubliners was "a chapter of the moral history of my country and I chose Dublin for the scene because that city seemed to me the centre of paralysis." Every story in Dubliners dramatises a different form of paralysis: paralysis through religion, through drink, through nostalgia, through cowardice, through false respectability.

The characters see escape ("Eveline" is about to board a ship to escape to Argentina) but cannot take it. They are frozen by fear, habit, obligation, or simply by the weight of the world they inhabit.

Eveline's paralysis

Eveline is about to emigrate to Buenos Aires with Frank, a sailor who loves her. On the quay, as the ship is about to sail, she cannot board. She clings to the barrier with both hands. Frank calls to her; she does not move. "She set her white face to him, passive, like a helpless animal." Ireland holds her — or she holds herself.

Joyce does not judge her; he shows the mechanism of paralysis with clinical precision.

Theme 02

Epiphany — the moment of revelation

Joyce's definition

In Stephen Hero (the early draft of A Portrait), Joyce defines epiphany as "a sudden spiritual manifestation, whether in the vulgarity of speech or of gesture or in a memorable phase of the mind itself... the most delicate and evanescent of moments." The epiphany is not a dramatic event — it is a small, often trivial moment that suddenly illuminates a larger truth.

In "Araby" the epiphany comes when the boy sees himself "as a creature driven and derided by vanity." In "The Dead," Gabriel's epiphany comes when he hears of Michael Furey — he suddenly sees how he has never truly loved. In each case, a small revelation strips away illusion and reveals a harder truth. The epiphany is often painful — it is a form of disillusionment.

Style

Joyce's literary style

  • Scrupulous meanness — plain, spare prose in Dubliners; no ornament, all irony embedded in situation
  • Stream of consciousness — interior monologue in Ulysses; Molly Bloom as its extreme
  • The mythic method — parallel with Homer gives Ulysses its structural irony
  • Multiple styles — each Ulysses episode has its own distinctive prose style
  • Language play — Finnegans Wake: puns across multiple languages simultaneously

T.S. Eliot on the mythic method

T.S. Eliot wrote that Joyce's use of the Odyssey as a parallel structure for Ulysses was "a way of controlling, of ordering, of giving a shape and a significance to the immense panorama of futility and anarchy which is contemporary history." The mythic method — using classical myths as underlying structures — became a key technique of Modernism (Eliot used it in The Waste Land).
