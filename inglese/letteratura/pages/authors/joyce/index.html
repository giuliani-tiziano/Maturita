James Joyce

Dublin, 1882 · Zurich, 1941 — The revolutionary of the novel

Section 01

Life — the essentials

1882
Born — Dublin
Born in Rathgar, Dublin, into a middle-class Catholic family. His father John was charming but irresponsible — the family fell steadily from relative comfort into poverty. Joyce received an excellent Jesuit education at Clongowes Wood College and Belvedere College.
1898–1902
University College Dublin
Studied modern languages. Began writing, met W.B. Yeats. Rejected the Irish Literary Revival — he found its nationalism and romanticism too limiting. Left for Paris, intending to study medicine, but spent his time writing and reading in the Bibliothèque Sainte-Geneviève.
1904
The decisive year — Nora and exile
Met Nora Barnacle, who became his lifelong companion (they married in 1931). On 16 June 1904 — later immortalised as "Bloomsday" — they had their first date. Joyce left Ireland permanently, settling in Trieste, Zurich and Paris. He returned to Dublin only twice in the remaining 37 years of his life.
1914
Dubliners published
Fifteen short stories about Dublin life, written in a style of "scrupulous meanness." Rejected by 22 publishers over nine years. The collection ends with "The Dead," widely considered one of the greatest short stories in the English language.
1916
A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man
Autobiographical Bildungsroman following Stephen Dedalus. Introduced free indirect discourse and the "epiphany" as a literary technique. Stephen's famous declaration: "I will not serve that in which I no longer believe."
1922
Ulysses — the summit of Modernism
Published by Sylvia Beach's Shakespeare and Company in Paris. Seven years in the writing. Set entirely on 16 June 1904 in Dublin. Uses Homer's Odyssey as a parallel structure. Banned in the USA until 1933. T.S. Eliot called it "the most important expression which the present age has found."
1939
Finnegans Wake
Joyce's final and most experimental work: written over 17 years, it uses a multi-layered, punning language blending many tongues. Set entirely during a dream. Many consider it unreadable; others consider it the supreme achievement in English prose.

Key vocabulary

Epiphany
Joyce's term (from theology): a sudden spiritual revelation triggered by a small, ordinary detail — the central mechanism of Dubliners.
Stream of consciousness
Narrative technique recording the uninterrupted flow of thought — Molly Bloom's monologue in Ulysses is its supreme example.
Paralysis
Joyce's diagnosis of Dublin and Ireland: moral, social and spiritual stagnation — the underlying theme of all Dubliners stories.
Bloomsday
16 June 1904 — the day on which Ulysses is set; celebrated globally every year.
