Comparisons

Joyce vs Woolf · Joyce vs Beckett · Joyce vs Dickens

Comparison 01

Joyce vs Woolf — Paralysis vs consciousness

Joyce — Dublin as paralysis

Joyce's characters are trapped by their city, their religion, their nationality. The Dubliners stories are about people who cannot leave — literally (Eveline) or spiritually (The Dead). Even Joyce himself had to leave Dublin to write about it. Exile is the condition of artistic freedom.

Woolf — London as consciousness

Woolf's London is not a trap but a texture: the streets of Westminster, the shops, the parties are the material through which consciousness moves. For Clarissa Dalloway, the city is a source of richness and connection, not paralysis. The city enables rather than imprisons.

Comparison 02

Joyce vs Beckett — The Irish Modernist legacy

Joyce — language as richness

Joyce's Ulysses and Finnegans Wake expand language to its maximum: puns across multiple languages, portmanteau words, multi-layered allusions. Language is the medium of abundance — everything can be said, and said many ways at once.

Beckett — language as failure

Beckett's response to Joyce was to go in the opposite direction: strip language down, until it fails. "I realised that Joyce was on the way up and I was on the way down." Beckett's art is about what cannot be said, what language fails to capture. Both worked in exile from Ireland; both wrote in French.

Comparison 03

Joyce vs Dickens — Two cities, two methods

Dickens's London

Dickens's London is vivid, populated and socially stratified. Characters represent social types (the miser, the fallen woman, the orphan). The city's injustice is the subject — the reader is invited to feel outrage and sympathy. The narrative voice is omniscient and morally engaged.

Joyce's Dublin

Joyce's Dublin is rendered through consciousness — the city is experienced from inside a particular mind at a particular moment. Characters are not social types but specific, singular consciousnesses. There is no moral narrator; the story implies judgment through irony and structure. The method is radically different: Dickens shows, Joyce implies.

"Think you're escaping and run into yourself." — James Joyce, Ulysses
