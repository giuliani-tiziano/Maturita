Comparisons

Blake vs Wordsworth · Blake vs Dickens · Blake vs Orwell

Comparison 01

Blake vs Wordsworth — Two Romantics, two responses to nature

Blake — nature as symbol of spiritual vision

Blake's nature is never purely physical — a grain of sand contains a world; a flower contains heaven. Nature is a doorway to visionary experience. But Blake is suspicious of nature worship: he saw it as another form of materialism. He is the poet of the city as much as of nature — and his city is a place of oppression.

Wordsworth — nature as teacher and healer

Wordsworth's nature is a living moral force — it shapes the child's character, heals adult suffering, restores lost connection. He experienced nature mystically but described it naturalistically. Blake found Wordsworth's nature poetry too passive and insufficiently visionary: "Natural Objects always did and now do weaken, deaden and obliterate Imagination in Me."

Comparison 02

Blake vs Dickens — The Industrial Revolution

Blake — visionary prophet (1790s)

Blake attacked the Industrial Revolution half a century before it fully developed, using symbolic poetry and visionary myth. His "dark Satanic Mills" and chimney sweeps show an artist who saw the horror before most people recognised it. His method is symbolic and prophetic.

Dickens — social reformer (1840s–60s)

Dickens attacks the same industrial exploitation — child labour, factory conditions, the workhouse — with documentary realism and emotional narrative. Where Blake uses a child chimney sweep in 16 lines of symbolic poetry, Dickens uses Oliver Twist in 366 pages of realistic narrative. Same target; radically different method.

Comparison 03

Blake vs Orwell — The "mind-forg'd manacles"

Blake (1794)

"Mind-forg'd manacles" — in 1794, Blake identified that the most effective oppression is psychological: the mind imprisons itself. Political freedom means nothing if the mind accepts its chains. This is a Romantic insight that anticipates modern ideas of ideology and false consciousness.

Orwell (1949)

Doublethink in Nineteen Eighty-Four is the totalitarian version of "mind-forg'd manacles": the Party's ultimate achievement is not merely controlling behaviour but restructuring consciousness so that the victim accepts and loves their oppression. Winston must love Big Brother. Blake prophesied this 150 years earlier.
