William Blake · Works

Major Works

Songs of Innocence and Experience — two contrary states of the soul

Masterwork

Songs of Innocence and of Experience (1789 / 1794)

Blake published Songs of Innocence (1789) and Songs of Experience (1794) as a single volume with the subtitle: "Shewing the Two Contrary States of the Human Soul." Many poems appear in pairs — the same title, the same surface subject, but utterly different in tone and implication:

Pairs of poems — key contrasts

The Lamb (Innocence)
Simple, childlike; the lamb is gentle Christ; the speaker is a child; God is gentle and loving
The Tyger (Experience)
Awe and fear; the tiger's fearful symmetry implies a creator capable of evil as well as good
The Chimney Sweeper (Innocence)
Child swept dreams of freedom; God and angels will "wash them clean"; false consolation
The Chimney Sweeper (Experience)
No dream; a child in snow; parents "gone to church to praise God" while their child is exploited; bitter irony
Holy Thursday (Innocence)
Charity children singing; a vision of beauty
Holy Thursday (Experience)
"Is this a holy thing to see... In a rich and fruitful land?"— anger at poverty amid wealth

The Tyger — the central question

"Tyger Tyger, burning bright,
In the forests of the night;
What immortal hand or eye,
Could frame thy fearful symmetry?"

The question at the heart of the poem: what kind of creator made both the gentle lamb and the terrible tiger? If God made the lamb, how could the same God make the tiger? The poem does not answer — it sits with the unanswerable. Blake was the first English poet to make the problem of evil into great lyric poetry.

Illuminated printing

Each copy of the Songs is unique — Blake printed the copper plates, then hand-coloured each copy. The images and text together form the complete poem. In some copies the Tyger looks terrifying; in others it looks almost comical — Blake deliberately varied the images to suggest multiple readings.

"Tyger Tyger, burning bright, / In the forests of the night." — William Blake, Songs of Experience (1794)
