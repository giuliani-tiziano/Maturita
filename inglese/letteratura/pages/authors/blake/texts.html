Key Texts

Annotated poems for the Maturità oral exam

Text 01

The Tyger (Songs of Experience, 1794)

Tyger Tyger, burning bright,
In the forests of the night;
What immortal hand or eye,
Could frame thy fearful symmetry?

In what distant deeps or skies,
Burnt the fire of thine eyes?
On what wings dare he aspire?
What the hand, dare seize the fire?

And what shoulder, & what art,
Could twist the sinews of thy heart?
And when thy heart began to beat,
What dread hand? & what dread feet?

Did he smile his work to see?
Did he who made the Lamb make thee?

The central question

"Did he who made the Lamb make thee?" — this is the theological core. If God made the gentle lamb (Christ, Innocence) he also made the fearsome tiger. How can a good God create evil? Blake does not answer — he accumulates questions, never resolving them. The unresolved question is the poem's power.

"Fearful symmetry" — the tiger is both terrible and beautiful; its danger and its perfection are the same thing. Blake was the first to see this.

Form and meaning

  • Trochaic tetrameter — "TY-ger TY-ger BURN-ing BRIGHT" — hammer blows, powerful and relentless
  • Questions only — no answers; the poem enacts the unresolvable
  • "Dare seize the fire" — Promethean allusion: who dares steal divine fire?
  • Repetition of "What" — each stanza asks a new unanswerable question
  • Final change — last stanza: "Could" → "Dare" — the question becomes more audacious
Text 02

London (Songs of Experience, 1794)

I wander thro' each charter'd street,
Near where the charter'd Thames does flow,
And mark in every face I meet
Marks of weakness, marks of woe.

In every cry of every Man,
In every Infant's cry of fear,
In every voice, in every ban,
The mind-forg'd manacles I hear.

How the Chimney-sweeper's cry
Every black'ning Church appals;
And the hapless Soldier's sigh
Runs in blood down Palace walls.

But most thro' midnight streets I hear
How the youthful Harlot's curse
Blasts the new-born Infant's tear,
And blights with plagues the Marriage hearse.

Key phrases

  • "charter'd" — twice in the opening; everything is owned, commodified, controlled
  • "mind-forg'd manacles" — the most famous Blake phrase: oppression is not only external (chains) but internal (the mind imprisons itself through fear and conformity)
  • "black'ning Church" — the Church is blackened by industrial soot AND by its moral failure — it stands silent while chimney sweeps die
  • "Marriage hearse" — oxymoron: marriage = life becomes death; the harlot's disease (syphilis) destroys the bourgeois institution of marriage

Social critique

The four stanzas of "London" contain the four pillars of Blake's social critique: (1) the market (charter'd streets); (2) mental oppression (mind-forg'd manacles); (3) the failure of Church and State (Church/Palace); (4) the corruption of sexuality by poverty (the harlot). Together they form a diagnosis of a society in which every institution has failed.
