William Blake

London, 1757 · London, 1827 — Visionary poet and engraver

Section 01

Life — the essentials

1757
Born — London, Soho
Born in Soho, London, to a hosier. His family were Dissenters — non-conformist Protestants — and Blake never attended school or university, being self-educated. He claimed to have had visions from childhood: at age 4 he saw God's head at the window; at 8 he saw angels in a tree at Peckham Rye.
1772–1779
Apprentice engraver
Apprenticed to engraver James Basire for seven years. Engraving became Blake's primary trade throughout his life — he supported himself by engraving illustrations for books and periodicals. This craft shaped his unique method of "illuminated printing" which combined text and image.
1783
Poetical Sketches
His first poetry collection, published with the help of friends. Shows the influence of Shakespeare, Milton and the Bible. Not commercially successful — most copies were given away.
1789
Songs of Innocence
Published using his innovative relief etching technique: he wrote the text and drew the illustrations on copper plates, printed them, then hand-coloured each copy. Songs of Innocence presents an idealised, childlike world — a vision of the divine in nature and childhood.
1789
French Revolution — radical sympathies
Blake supported the French and American Revolutions and wore a red cap (the bonnet rouge) in the streets of London. He was a fierce opponent of monarchy, organised religion, and industrial capitalism. He was tried for sedition in 1803 (acquitted).
1794
Songs of Experience
The companion volume to Songs of Innocence. Blake published them together as Songs of Innocence and of Experience: Shewing the Two Contrary States of the Human Soul. Where Innocence sees the divine and hopeful, Experience reveals corruption, exploitation and oppression — including child labour.
1827
Death — Fountain Court, London
Died singing of the things he saw in heaven. Almost unknown during his lifetime (he sold very few books), Blake was rediscovered in the 19th century and is now regarded as one of the great English poets and visual artists.

Key vocabulary

Innocence vs Experience
Blake's fundamental dialectic: two "contrary states" of the human soul — the child's innocent wonder (pre-fall) and the adult's experience of corruption and suffering.
The Lamb / The Tyger
Blake's two most famous symbols: the Lamb = Christ, gentleness, innocence; the Tyger = energy, power, the mystery of evil and creation.
"Dark Satanic Mills"
Blake's phrase from the preface to Milton: refers to the factories of the Industrial Revolution, which he saw as destroying the human spirit.
Illuminated printing
Blake's unique technique of combining hand-written text and engraved images on the same copper plate, then hand-colouring each print.
