William Blake · Themes & Style

Themes & Style

Innocence vs Experience · Oppression · The Industrial Revolution · Vision

Theme 01

Innocence vs Experience — the two contrary states

Blake does not present Innocence as simply good and Experience as simply bad. Both are necessary states — neither is sufficient alone. Innocence without experience is naive and vulnerable; Experience without Innocence is bitter and cynical. Blake calls them "contrary states": opposites that together produce the full truth.

This dialectic — thesis vs antithesis seeking synthesis — is one of Blake's most modern insights. The child who has never suffered and the adult who has forgotten joy are both incomplete.

The "Nurse's Song" — the contrast

In Innocence, the nurse calls the children in from play at nightfall — with reluctance, and gentle; the children continue playing happily. In Experience, the same scenario: but the nurse's face is "pale and green" — jealous of the children's joy she has lost. The same situation reveals two emotional worlds: one open, one closed.

Theme 02

Social oppression — child labour and the Industrial Revolution

"London" — the city of oppression

"I wander thro' each charter'd street,
Near where the charter'd Thames does flow.
And mark in every face I meet
Marks of weakness, marks of woe."

"Charter'd" = controlled, commodified — even the river is owned. "Marks of weakness, marks of woe" — the triple repetition of "marks" is both physical (scars, brands on the face) and spiritual (the marks of oppression written on the soul). Blake sees a city of controlled suffering.

The "dark Satanic Mills" of Blake's preface to Milton are the factories of the Industrial Revolution — which Blake saw destroying both the natural landscape and the human spirit. Child labourers (chimney sweeps, factory children) appear repeatedly in the Songs. Blake was writing before any factory legislation — children could legally work twelve-hour days in dangerous conditions.

Style

Blake's literary style

  • Simple ballad form — short lines, regular rhyme, accessible to children in Innocence
  • Irony — surface simplicity concealing bitter social critique in Experience
  • Symbolism — the Lamb, the Tyger, the rose, the sunflower all carry complex symbolic weight
  • Repetition — "Marks of weakness, marks of woe" — incantatory, intensifying effect
  • Vision and prophecy — the longer prophetic books use a very different style: long, visionary, complex

Blake as a Pre-Romantic

Blake shared the Romantics' rejection of Enlightenment rationalism and industrialism. But he went further: he created his own mythological universe (the Zoas, Albion, Urizen = reason as tyrant) and never joined any literary group or movement. He was prophetic and solitary — recognising the forces that would dominate the 19th century before anyone else.

"I must create a system or be enslaved by another man's." — William Blake, Jerusalem
