William Wordsworth

Cumberland, 1770 · Westmorland, 1850 — Poet of nature and memory

Section 01

Life — the essentials

1770
Born — Cockermouth, Cumberland
Born in Cockermouth in the Lake District. His mother died when he was eight; his father when he was thirteen. These losses, combined with a childhood of extraordinary freedom in the landscape of the Lakes, formed his poetic sensibility and his belief in the healing power of nature.
1787–1791
Cambridge and France
Studied at St John's College, Cambridge. Walked through France and the Alps in 1790. Returned to France in 1791–1792 during the Revolution — he was initially enthusiastic. Fell in love with Annette Vallon, who bore him a daughter (Anne-Caroline) before he was forced to leave France as England and France went to war.
1795
Friendship with Coleridge
Met Samuel Taylor Coleridge — the most important friendship of his life and a creative partnership that would transform English poetry. They lived near each other in Somerset. Their conversations led directly to the Lyrical Ballads.
1798
Lyrical Ballads — the Romantic manifesto
Published jointly with Coleridge. The 1800 Preface (second edition) is the founding document of English Romanticism: it declares poetry should use "the real language of men" and deal with "incidents and situations from common life." Wordsworth contributed 19 poems; Coleridge 4 (including The Rime of the Ancient Mariner).
1799–1814
Grasmere — Dove Cottage
Settled at Dove Cottage, Grasmere, with his sister Dorothy. Wrote his greatest poetry here: "Tintern Abbey," the "Immortality Ode," the early books of The Prelude. Married Mary Hutchinson in 1802. His sister Dorothy's Grasmere Journal shows how closely she collaborated in the creative process.
1850
Death and The Prelude published
His autobiographical poem The Prelude — over 8,000 lines tracing the "growth of a Poet's mind" from childhood to maturity — was published after his death. It had been revised over 50 years. He was Poet Laureate from 1843. Died at 80 — the only major Romantic poet to reach old age.

Key vocabulary

The "spots of time"
Wordsworth's term for formative childhood experiences preserved in memory, which continue to nourish the adult imagination.
Spots of nature
Specific landscapes (mountains, rivers, trees) that act as emotional anchors connecting past and present consciousness.
Recollection in tranquillity
From the Preface to Lyrical Ballads: poetry originates in "emotion recollected in tranquillity" — not immediate experience but its later, meditative processing.
The Sublime
The aesthetic experience of overwhelming grandeur in nature — awe mixed with terror; Wordsworth's mountain landscapes epitomise it.
