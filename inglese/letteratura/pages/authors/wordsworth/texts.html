Annotated passages for the Maturità oral exam

Text 01

"I Wandered Lonely as a Cloud" (1807)

I wandered lonely as a cloud
That floats on high o'er vales and hills,
When all at once I saw a crowd,
A host, of golden daffodils;
Beside the lake, beneath the trees,
Fluttering and dancing in the breeze.

Continuous as the stars that shine
And twinkle on the milky way,
They stretched in never-ending line
Along the margin of a bay:
Ten thousand saw I at a glance,
Tossing their heads in sprightly dance.

For oft, when on my couch I lie
In vacant or in pensive mood,
They flash upon that inward eye
Which is the bliss of solitude;
And then my heart with pleasure fills,
And dances with the daffodils.

Nature and memory

The poem is not primarily about the daffodils — it is about the memory of the daffodils. The key is the final stanza: "oft, when on my couch I lie... They flash upon that inward eye." The real poem begins in the future — when the speaker is alone in a city, the memory of the daffodils spontaneously restores his joy. Nature's gift is not immediate pleasure but a lasting treasury of restored emotion.

Style — language of common speech

  • Simple diction — no classical allusions, no elevated vocabulary
  • ABABCC rhyme scheme — easy, natural, not forced
  • Similes from the everyday — "as a cloud," "as the stars"
  • "Inward eye" — the imagination that stores and replays experience; Wordsworth's key concept
  • "Bliss of solitude" — paradox: the daffodils in a crowd fill the solitary mind
Text 02

Tintern Abbey — the three ages (extract, 1798)

"For I have learned
To look on nature, not as in the hour
Of thoughtless youth; but hearing oftentimes
The still, sad music of humanity,
Nor harsh nor grating, though of ample power
To chasten and subdue. And I have felt
A presence that disturbs me with the joy
Of elevated thoughts; a sense sublime
Of something far more deeply interfused,
Whose dwelling is the light of setting suns,
And the round ocean and the living air,
And the blue sky, and in the mind of man;
A motion and a spirit, that impels
All thinking things, all objects of all thought,
And rolls through all things."

The Romantic sublime

"A sense sublime / Of something far more deeply interfused" — Wordsworth cannot name this "something" precisely. It dwells in nature (light of setting suns, the ocean, the sky) AND "in the mind of man." It is a pantheistic spirit — not the Christian God of a church but a divine presence immanent in the natural world. This is Romantic spirituality: not supernatural but natural.

The three ages — from the poem

This passage marks the third stage of Wordsworth's relationship with nature: maturity. He no longer runs through the hills with "aching joys" (youth) — now he hears "the still, sad music of humanity." Experience has given him depth at the cost of immediacy. The mature poet gains moral understanding; loses wild joy. This is the Romantic version of the Fortunate Fall.

"And then my heart with pleasure fills, / And dances with the daffodils." — William Wordsworth, I Wandered Lonely as a Cloud
