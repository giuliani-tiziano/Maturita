William Wordsworth · Themes & Style

Themes & Style

Nature · Memory · Childhood · The Sublime

Theme 01

Nature as moral teacher

For Wordsworth, nature is not merely scenery — it is a living moral force. The mountains, rivers and lakes of the Lake District actively shape the child's character: "Wisdom and Spirit of the Universe! ... I grew up / Fostered alike by beauty and by fear." The landscape teaches lessons that human society cannot: patience, humility, proportion, the sense of something larger than the self.

Nature also heals. After the depression of his years in London and the disappointment of the French Revolution, returning to the natural landscape of the Wye Valley restored Wordsworth's sense of moral order — as described in "Tintern Abbey."

The "spots of time"

In The Prelude, Wordsworth describes certain moments in childhood — often frightening encounters with nature — that became permanently formative: a stolen boat, a night of ice-skating, a bird-trap. These "spots of time" continue to nourish the adult imagination long after the experience. Memory is not passive storage but active creative force.

"There are in our existence spots of time, / That with distinct pre-eminence retain / A renovating virtue." — The Prelude
Theme 02

Childhood and lost innocence

The "Immortality Ode" (1804)

Full title: "Ode: Intimations of Immortality from Recollections of Early Childhood." Its central argument: the child comes "trailing clouds of glory" from a pre-existence in Heaven. As we grow up we lose this divine light. The adult can only recall it through memory and imagination — but cannot recapture it. The loss of childhood is the fall from grace.

"The Rainbow comes and goes, / And lovely is the Rose... / But yet I know, where'er I go, / That there hath past away a glory from the earth."

This theme connects Wordsworth's Romanticism to Blake's Innocence/Experience dialectic. Both see childhood as a state of greater perception and spiritual access. For Blake, Experience corrupts; for Wordsworth, it enriches but also diminishes. The Romantic cult of childhood is central to both poets.

Style

Wordsworth's literary style

  • Blank verse — the primary medium for meditative poetry (Tintern Abbey, The Prelude)
  • "Real language of men" — simple diction, avoiding "poetic" archaism
  • Long, meditative syntax — sentences that unfold gradually, following the movement of thought
  • The ballad form — for shorter narrative poems about common people
  • Nature imagery — precise observation of specific landscapes, plants, weather

Wordsworth vs Pope — two styles

The 18th-century poet Alexander Pope wrote in heroic couplets (pairs of rhyming iambic pentameter lines), with polished, witty, artificial diction. Wordsworth's style is its opposite: unrhymed, irregular in length, drawing on everyday speech. This was a deliberate revolt against Augustan artifice in favour of democratic, natural expression.

"Poetry is the spontaneous overflow of powerful feelings: it takes its origin from emotion recollected in tranquillity." — Wordsworth, Preface to Lyrical Ballads
