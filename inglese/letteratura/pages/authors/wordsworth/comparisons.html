Comparisons

Wordsworth vs Blake · Wordsworth vs Byron · Wordsworth vs Woolf

Comparison 01

Wordsworth vs Byron — First and Second Generation Romantics

Wordsworth — nature, meditation, consolation

First-generation Romantic (with Coleridge). Nature is a moral teacher and healer. The poet's self is explored but ultimately reconciled with nature and humanity. Political disappointment (French Revolution) leads to retreat into the personal and natural. Conservative in later life — became Poet Laureate.

Byron — rebellion, passion, exile

Second-generation Romantic (with Keats and Shelley). The poet as rebel and outcast — the Byronic Hero who defies society. Nature is the backdrop to passion and adventure, not a moral teacher. Byron despised Wordsworth's conservatism: called him "Turdsworth." Byron died for a political cause; Wordsworth became establishment.

Comparison 02

Wordsworth vs Woolf — Memory and time

Wordsworth's "emotion recollected in tranquillity"

The creative process: intense emotion is first lived, then re-examined from a distance. Memory is the bridge between experience and art. The "spots of time" — formative childhood moments — nourish the adult imagination. Time allows emotion to be transformed into insight.

Woolf's involuntary memory

Like Proust, Woolf's characters experience involuntary memory — triggered by sensory detail without conscious intention. Clarissa Dalloway hears a squeak and is instantly in Bourton in 1890. This is closer to Bergson's durée than to Wordsworth's deliberate recollection. Both use memory as the site of meaning; the mechanism differs.

Comparison 03

Wordsworth vs Eliot — Nature and the Waste Land

Wordsworth's nature (1798)

Nature is healing, moral, divine. "Come forth into the light of things, / Let Nature be your teacher." The natural landscape restores what urban life depletes. Nature and the human soul are in harmony.

Eliot's Waste Land (1922)

"April is the cruellest month, breeding / Lilacs out of the dead land." For Eliot, nature's spring is an irony — the dead land cannot regenerate the dead souls of post-war civilisation. Where Wordsworth's nature heals, Eliot's nature mocks. The 130-year gap between the two visions measures the spiritual distance of Modernism from Romanticism.
