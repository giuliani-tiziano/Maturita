William Wordsworth · Works

Major Works

Lyrical Ballads — the founding document of English Romanticism

Key Work I

Lyrical Ballads (1798, with Coleridge)

The 1798 first edition contained 23 poems: 19 by Wordsworth, 4 by Coleridge (including The Rime of the Ancient Mariner). The 1800 second edition added Wordsworth's famous Preface — the founding document of English Romanticism.

The Preface (1800) — key claims

"The real language of men"
Poetry should use ordinary speech, not elevated "poetic diction" — democratising literature
"Incidents from common life"
The subjects of poetry should be ordinary people (shepherds, farmers, outcasts) not classical heroes
"Emotion recollected in tranquillity"
The creative process: intense feeling is first experienced, then re-examined calmly — producing poetry
"The spontaneous overflow of powerful feelings"
But emotion must have been "thought long and deeply" before it becomes art

Key poems in Lyrical Ballads

  • "Lines Written a Few Miles Above Tintern Abbey" — meditation on memory, nature and the "spots of time"
  • "The Tables Turned" — "Come forth into the light of things, / Let Nature be your teacher"
  • "Lines Written in Early Spring" — "What man has made of man" — the contrast between nature's harmony and human cruelty
  • "The Thorn" — Gothic ballad of infanticide; "real language of men" experiment
Key Work II

"Lines Composed a Few Miles Above Tintern Abbey" (1798)

Structure and argument

Written in blank verse. Wordsworth revisits the Wye Valley near Tintern Abbey five years after his first visit. He reflects on how the memory of the landscape has sustained him during his years in London. He distinguishes three phases of his relationship with nature:

  1. Wild childhood — pure animal pleasure, no reflection
  2. Youth — passionate, physical love of landscape
  3. Maturity — contemplative; "still, sad music of humanity"; nature as moral teacher

The poem ends as a dedication to his sister Dorothy — she represents the younger self he has moved beyond. He gives her his own past as a gift: "in thy voice I catch / The language of my former heart."

"I have learned
To look on nature, not as in the hour
Of thoughtless youth; but hearing oftentimes
The still, sad music of humanity." — Wordsworth, Tintern Abbey (1798)
"Come forth into the light of things, / Let Nature be your teacher." — William Wordsworth, The Tables Turned
