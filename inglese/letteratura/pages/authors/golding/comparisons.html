William Golding · Comparisons

Comparisons

Golding vs Shelley · Golding vs Orwell · Golding vs Dickens

Comparison 01

Golding vs Mary Shelley — Society and the making of evil

Shelley — evil is produced by society

Frankenstein's creature is born innocent and becomes violent only after being rejected by every human it encounters. Society — its cruelty, its refusal of empathy, its inability to accept difference — makes the monster monstrous. The creature reads Paradise Lost: like Satan, it was created good and made evil by its creator's abandonment. Evil has a cause: social rejection.

Golding — evil is innate

The boys in Lord of the Flies have not been socially rejected or abused — they are middle-class British schoolboys with every social advantage. They turn savage because humans are savage by nature. For Golding, Shelley's argument is dangerously optimistic: it implies that the right social conditions could eliminate evil. WWII proved, for Golding, that this is false.

Comparison 02

Golding vs Orwell — The structure of evil

Orwell — evil is institutional

In Orwell's dystopias (Animal Farm, 1984), evil is organised and systematic. The pigs take power by controlling language and information; the Party maintains power through surveillance and torture. Evil requires an apparatus — a Party, a Squealer, a Ministry of Truth. Individuals can resist; the system is the problem.

Golding — evil is spontaneous

Jack needs no party apparatus to become a tyrant — he only needs the removal of adult authority and a choir of followers. The descent is fast and requires no propaganda: the boys want to hunt, to paint their faces, to kill. For Golding, Orwell's institutional explanation is too comfortable: the boys on the island have no Ministry of Truth. The darkness is self-generating.

Comparison 03

Golding vs Dickens — The child as moral measure

Dickens — the innocent child

Oliver Twist's innate goodness survives despite the workhouse, Fagin's gang and the social system's best efforts to corrupt him. Children, for Dickens, are the innocent victims of an adult world — the measure of that world's moral failure. The child sees clearly; the adult has been corrupted by society.

Golding — the child as savage

Golding explicitly sets out to refute the Victorian sentimental belief in childhood innocence. Remove the adult framework and children do not reveal an innocent nature — they reveal a savage one. Roger, aged twelve, kills Piggy without compunction. The child in Golding is not a moral measure — the child is the raw material that civilization has always had to contain and suppress.

"I'm part of you. Close, close, close! I'm the reason why it's no go." — The Lord of the Flies, William Golding
