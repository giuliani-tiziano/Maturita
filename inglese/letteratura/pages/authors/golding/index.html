William Golding

Cornwall, 1911 · Wiltshire, 1993 — The beast within

Section 01

Life — the essentials

1911
Born — St Columb Minor, Cornwall
Born into an academic family: his father Alec was a science teacher and rationalist who believed in the perfectibility of humanity through reason and education. This optimistic worldview became the target of Golding's life's work.
1930–1934
Oxford — science then English
Initially studied natural sciences at Brasenose College, Oxford, before switching to English literature. After graduating, worked as a teacher of English and theatre for fourteen years at Bishop Wordsworth's School, Salisbury.
1940–1945
Royal Navy — World War II
Served as a lieutenant in the Royal Navy. Participated in the sinking of the Bismarck and D-Day. He later said the war revealed to him "the darkness of man's heart" — the defining phrase of Lord of the Flies. The experience destroyed his father's rational optimism.
1954
Lord of the Flies — rejected 21 times
Published by Faber & Faber after 21 rejections (one editor called it "an absurd and uninteresting fantasy"). Within a decade it became one of the most widely read English novels in the world, particularly in schools. It deliberately inverts R.M. Ballantyne's Victorian boys' adventure The Coral Island (1857).
1983
Nobel Prize in Literature
Awarded the Nobel Prize "for his novels which, with the perspicuity of realistic narrative art and the diversity and universality of myth, illuminate the human condition in the world of today." His other major works include The Spire (1964) and Rites of Passage (1980).
1993
Death — Perranarworthal, Cornwall
Died of heart failure at 81. He had described himself as a pessimist who had never entirely lost hope — an appropriate epitaph for the author of Lord of the Flies.

Key vocabulary

Original sin
The theological concept that evil is innate in human nature — Golding's secular counterpart to this is the central argument of Lord of the Flies.
The beast
In Lord of the Flies: what the boys believe is a monster on the island — actually, as Simon realises, the beast is within themselves.
Allegory
A narrative in which characters and events represent abstract ideas — Lord of the Flies allegorises the fragility of civilisation.
The coral island inversion
Golding's novel deliberately reverses the Victorian boys' adventure story: where Ballantyne's boys are moral and successful, Golding's descend into savagery.
