William Golding · Themes & Style

Themes & Style

Original sin · The beast · Civilisation vs savagery · Allegory

Theme 01

Original sin — the beast within

The central question of Lord of the Flies: where does the beast come from? The boys fear a literal beast on the island — a creature they have not seen. Simon alone understands: "Maybe... there is a beast... maybe it's only us." The Lord of the Flies speaks to Simon: "Fancy thinking the Beast was something you could hunt and kill! You knew, didn't you? I'm part of you." The beast is not external — it is innate to human nature.

This is the theological doctrine of original sin: humanity is not inherently good (Rousseau's "noble savage") but inherently fallen. Remove civilization and you do not reveal a natural innocence — you reveal the darkness that civilization was built to contain.

Simon as Christ figure

Simon alone perceives the truth about the beast. He goes into the jungle to meditate (like Christ in Gethsemane), discovers the Lord of the Flies (a pig's head on a stake, swarming with flies — Beelzebub, the devil), and hears it speak. He frees the dead parachutist that the boys thought was the beast. Coming back to tell them the truth, he is killed in a ritual dance — mistaken for the beast himself. The truth-teller is destroyed by the mob. The Christ parallel is deliberate.

Theme 02

Symbols of civilization and their destruction

The conch

The conch shell is the symbol of democratic order — whoever holds it has the right to speak in assembly. It represents law, reason, and political legitimacy. When Roger rolls the boulder that kills Piggy, the conch is simultaneously "exploded into a thousand white fragments." The destruction of the symbol and the death of the intellect happen in the same moment. No accident.

Piggy's glasses

The glasses allow the boys to start the signal fire (technology, reason, connection to civilization). Jack steals them for the hunters' fire (to roast pigs — pleasure, ritual). When they are finally smashed, Piggy's sight fails — literally and symbolically. Golding's imagery is precise: civilization requires clear-sightedness; savagery blinds.

Style

Golding's narrative technique

  • Allegorical structure — every element has a symbolic meaning beyond the literal; read like a fable
  • Close third-person — narration focalized through Ralph; but Golding maintains ironic distance
  • Nature descriptions — initially Edenic ("flower and fruit"), progressively darkening (rot, heat, blood)
  • Ironic ending — the naval officer sees children playing; cannot understand what he sees. The reader sees that adult war is continuous with the boys' savagery
"Fun and games... I should have thought that a pack of British boys — you're all British aren't you? — would have been able to put up a better show than that." — The naval officer, Lord of the Flies
"Maybe there is a beast... maybe it's only us." — Simon, Lord of the Flies
