William Golding · Works

Major Works

Lord of the Flies — civilization stripped away

Masterwork

Lord of the Flies (1954)

A group of British schoolboys are evacuated from a nuclear war and crash-land on a tropical island. There are no adults. At first they attempt to organise themselves democratically (Ralph is elected leader; the conch shell is the symbol of order and the right to speak; Piggy's glasses start the signal fire). Gradually, led by Jack and his hunters, they descend into tribal savagery: Simon is killed in a ritual frenzy; Piggy is murdered; the conch is smashed. Only Ralph survives, weeping for "the end of innocence and the darkness of man's heart." A naval officer arrives to rescue them — but the officer is from a warship, suggesting the savagery continues in the adult world.

Key characters and their symbolic roles

Ralph
Democracy, civilisation, reason — elected leader who tries to maintain order
Jack
Savagery, tribalism, the will to power — becomes chief of the hunters
Piggy
Intellect, science, rational society — killed when the conch is smashed
Simon
Spiritual insight, innate goodness — discovers the truth about the beast; killed as a scapegoat
Roger
Pure sadism — kills Piggy; represents evil beyond any social function
The Lord of the Flies
A pig's head on a stick; symbol of the beast within; "Beelzebub" = Lord of the Flies in Hebrew
Intertextuality

The Coral Island — inverted

R.M. Ballantyne's The Coral Island (1857)

Ballantyne's popular Victorian novel features three British boys stranded on a tropical island — they display all the virtues of British Christian culture and triumph over the "savage" natives. It is an imperialist fantasy: civilization is inherent in the British boy. Golding takes the same scenario and reverses its moral completely. The boys' names in the two books are the same (Ralph, Jack, Peterkin/Simon) — the allusion is deliberate.

Golding's counter-argument

If Ballantyne's premise is correct — that civilisation is innate in British schoolboys — then remove adult authority and the boys should still build a functioning society. Golding's experiment says no: without the framework of civilization, the boys recreate tribalism and murder. The "savage" of Victorian adventure fiction was never abroad; he was always here, waiting for the rules to be removed.

"Ralph wept for the end of innocence, the darkness of man's heart." — William Golding, Lord of the Flies
