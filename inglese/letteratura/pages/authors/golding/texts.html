Text 01

Simon and the Lord of the Flies (Chapter 8)

Fancy thinking the Beast was something you could hunt and kill! You knew, didn't you? I'm part of you? Close, close, close! I'm the reason why it's no go? Why things are what they are?

The half-shut eyes were dim with the infinite cynicism of adult life. They assured Simon that everything was a bad business.

"I know that."

"—there isn't anyone to help you. Only me. And I'm the Beast."

Simon's mouth laboured, brought forth audible words.

"Pig's head on a stick."

"Fancy thinking the Beast was something you could hunt and kill!" said the head. For a moment or two the forest and all the other dimly appreciated places echoed with the parody of laughter. "You knew, didn't you? I'm part of you?"

The heart of the novel

This is the theological and philosophical centre of Lord of the Flies. The "Lord of the Flies" (Beelzebub in Hebrew — the devil) speaks to Simon. But this is a hallucination: Simon is epileptic, and this is a seizure. Golding's point is precise: Simon hears what he already knows. The beast is not supernatural — it is psychological. It is "part of you." Evil is innate, not external.

Language and tone

  • Direct address — "You knew, didn't you?" — the beast speaks with intimate familiarity
  • "The infinite cynicism of adult life" — the pig's head represents adult knowledge of human evil; the children have always known it
  • "Pig's head on a stick" — Simon's rational response, naming the object; but the object is also the symbol
  • Repetition — "Fancy thinking the Beast was something you could hunt and kill" repeated — the statement cannot be escaped
Text 02

Ralph weeps — the ending (Chapter 12)

Ralph wept for the end of innocence, the darkness of man's heart, and the fall through the air of the true, wise friend called Piggy.

The officer, surrounded by these noises, was moved and a little embarrassed. He turned away to give them time to pull themselves together; and waited, allowing his eyes to rest on the trim cruiser in the distance.

The irony of rescue

The officer who "rescues" Ralph is a naval officer — from a warship, in a war. He is embarrassed by the crying children, turns away to look at "the trim cruiser." The cruiser is the adult equivalent of the boys' violence: organised, legitimate, equally deadly. The boys have not been saved from savagery — they have been returned to a larger and more efficient version of it. The novel ends without consolation.

"The end of innocence"

Ralph's weeping is for what he has understood — what Simon always knew. "The darkness of man's heart" is the novel's final statement. This is original sin not as doctrine but as experience: Ralph has lived through what theology describes. Golding, a teacher who had taught boys, wrote this in 1954, nine years after WWII. The phrase "end of innocence" echoes throughout post-war literature.

"Ralph wept for the end of innocence, the darkness of man's heart." — William Golding, Lord of the Flies
