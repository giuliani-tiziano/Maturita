Historical Context

An age of transition (1750–1790)

Pre-Romanticism designates the literary period between Augustan Neoclassicism (reason, order, wit) and full Romanticism (imagination, emotion, nature). It is marked by a growing dissatisfaction with pure reason, a new interest in ruins, melancholy, night-time, graves, and the wild landscape. The term was first used retrospectively — the poets of this period did not call themselves pre-Romantics.

Contextually, this is the period of the Enlightenment's philosophical confidence — but also of its first cracks: the Lisbon earthquake (1755) shook faith in divine providence; Rousseau's "noble savage" questioned European civilisation; Edmund Burke's Philosophical Enquiry into the Origin of our Ideas of the Sublime and Beautiful (1757) gave the movement its central aesthetic term.

Key Characteristics

The Pre-Romantic sensibility

The Graveyard School

A group of poets who contemplated death, ruins and night as vehicles for spiritual meditation. Key texts: Thomas Gray's Elegy Written in a Country Churchyard (1751) — the most read English poem of the 18th century; Edward Young's Night Thoughts (1742); Robert Blair's The Grave (1743). Common elements: the owl, the midnight, the yew tree, the anonymous grave. Death equalises all: "The paths of glory lead but to the grave."

The Sublime vs the Beautiful

Edmund Burke (1757): Beauty produces love and pleasure; the Sublime produces astonishment, terror and awe. Mountains, storms, ocean, night — these are sublime because they overwhelm the human capacity to comprehend them. The sublime is "delightful horror" — terror at a safe distance. This aesthetic gave the Pre-Romantics their fundamental term and laid the groundwork for Romantic nature poetry.

Sensibility and feeling

The cult of "sensibility" — acute emotional responsiveness — elevated feeling as a moral quality. The person of sensibility weeps at poverty, trembles at beauty, cannot bear cruelty. Sterne's A Sentimental Journey (1768), Richardson's Pamela — the novel itself as a form was born from this culture. Sensibility was both a genuine revaluation of emotional life and a social performance; Jane Austen later satirised its excesses in Sense and Sensibility (1811).

Ossian and the Celtic past

James Macpherson's Poems of Ossian (1760–63) claimed to be translations of ancient Gaelic poetry — in fact, largely invented. The controversy did not diminish their influence: they popularised melancholy, heroic death, wild Highland landscapes, and a romantic vision of the Celtic past across Europe. Napoleon carried a copy on his campaigns. The precedent of "forging" tradition to meet emotional needs is itself telling.

Key Text

Thomas Gray — Elegy Written in a Country Churchyard (1751)

"The curfew tolls the knell of parting day,
The lowing herd wind slowly o'er the lea,
The plowman homeward plods his weary way,
And leaves the world to darkness and to me." — Thomas Gray, stanza 1

The Elegy meditates on the anonymous lives of the village poor buried in a rural churchyard. Its central argument: the obscure graves of common people deserve as much attention as the monuments of the great. The poem is pre-Romantic in its melancholy, its rural setting, its elevation of the common life — but its meditative structure and balanced heroic quatrains still belong to Neoclassicism. It is a perfect threshold text.

Legacy

From Pre-Romanticism to Romanticism

The Pre-Romantics prepared the ground for Romantic poetry in several ways: they made emotion a legitimate literary subject; they introduced the landscape of terror and sublimity; they valorised the medieval, the folk, and the non-classical past; and they established the isolated, sensitive individual as the moral centre of literature. Blake, Wordsworth, Coleridge and Byron each built on these foundations — and each pushed much further.

Key term to know

The Sublime: an aesthetic experience characterised by awe, terror and the sense of being overwhelmed by something vastly greater than oneself. First theorised by Longinus (1st century AD), developed by Burke (1757), central to Kant's philosophy, and the dominant aesthetic of Romanticism. Distinct from Beauty (which pleases) — the Sublime astonishes and terrifies.
