Definition

Utopia inverted: the dystopian imagination

A dystopia (from Greek dys = bad + topos = place) is a fictional society that presents itself as ideal (or claims to be) but is in fact oppressive, dehumanising and unjust. It is the dark mirror of utopia (Thomas More, 1516). Dystopian fiction emerged as a literary genre in the 20th century as a response to the rise of totalitarian regimes (Soviet Communism, Nazi Fascism, Stalinist show trials) and the technological acceleration of the capacity to control populations. The genre asks: what happens when a government achieves perfect control?

Key Texts

The two great English dystopias

Aldous Huxley — Brave New World (1932)

The World State has eliminated unhappiness through technology and conditioning: babies are produced in bottles ("Bokanovsky's Process"), social class is predetermined, sex is recreational and mandatory, the drug "soma" suppresses any negative emotion. There is no violence, no religion, no family, no art, no freedom. Bernard Marx and Lenina Crowne visit a "Savage Reservation" — the last corner of natural humanity. John the Savage, brought to London, refuses to accept the "happiness" of the World State. His suicide is the dystopia's final act.

Huxley's dystopia: control through pleasure. Oppression without oppressors. The citizens want to be controlled.

George Orwell — Nineteen Eighty-Four (1949)

Airstrip One (formerly England), divided into three superstates perpetually at war. The Party, led by the never-seen Big Brother, controls everything: thought (Thoughtcrime), language (Newspeak is designed to make dissent impossible), history (the Ministry of Truth rewrites the past). Winston Smith works at the Ministry of Truth, falsifying records. He keeps a secret diary. He falls in love with Julia. He contacts O'Brien, who he believes is a secret rebel. The betrayal, torture and Room 101 follow.

Orwell's dystopia: control through fear. O'Brien: "If you want a picture of the future, imagine a boot stamping on a human face — for ever."

Key Concepts

Newspeak, doublethink, Big Brother

Newspeak
Orwell's invented language designed to make thoughtcrime impossible. Words are removed from vocabulary; "ungood" replaces "bad"; "crimethink" names disallowed thought. The Appendix describes how Newspeak will be fully implemented by 2050. If you cannot name a thought, you cannot think it.
Doublethink
"The power of holding two contradictory beliefs in one's mind simultaneously, and accepting both of them." "War is Peace. Freedom is Slavery. Ignorance is Strength." The Party slogans state contradictions and demand acceptance of both.
The Ministry of Truth
The ministry that falsifies the past so that the Party is always right. Winston's job: to rewrite old newspaper articles to match the current Party line. History has no independent existence.

Huxley vs Orwell — two models of control

Neil Postman (Amusing Ourselves to Death, 1985): "Orwell feared those who would ban books. Huxley feared there would be no reason to ban a book, for there would be no one who wanted to read one. Orwell feared those who would deprive us of information. Huxley feared those who would give us so much that we would be reduced to passivity." In the 21st century, Huxley's model (distraction through pleasure) has arguably proved more accurate than Orwell's (coercion through fear).

"War is Peace. Freedom is Slavery. Ignorance is Strength." — George Orwell, Nineteen Eighty-Four
