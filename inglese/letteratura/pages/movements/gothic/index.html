Origins

The birth of Gothic fiction (1764)

The Gothic novel emerged as a reaction against Enlightenment rationalism, exploring the irrational, the supernatural, and the dark corners of the human psyche. Horace Walpole's The Castle of Otranto (1764) is usually identified as the first Gothic novel — a medieval castle, supernatural portents, tyranny and mystery. The term "Gothic" referred originally to the medieval/northern European (as opposed to classical/Mediterranean) — by extension, everything that Neoclassicism excluded: darkness, superstition, excess, the irrational.

Key Features

Gothic conventions

  • The castle or ruined building — symbol of decayed aristocratic authority; labyrinthine passages, dungeons, secret rooms
  • The supernatural — ghosts, monsters, animated portraits; often ambiguous (real or psychological?)
  • The villain-hero — a dark, powerful, transgressive figure; precursor of the Byronic hero
  • The persecuted heroine — threatened by the villain; tests the limits of innocence
  • Extreme emotion — terror, horror, passion, madness; the body overwhelmed
  • The past haunting the present — hidden sins, ancestral crimes, buried secrets return
  • Pathetic fallacy — storms, darkness and weather mirror the psychological state

Terror vs Horror

Ann Radcliffe (1764–1823) — the most popular Gothic novelist — distinguished between terror and horror: terror is the anticipation of danger (sublime, elevating, exciting the imagination); horror is the confrontation with it (disgusting, paralyzing). Radcliffe always explained her supernatural events rationally at the end ("the explained supernatural"). Matthew Lewis (The Monk, 1796) rejected this and used genuine supernatural horror — darker, more disturbing, more influenced by continental Gothic.

Key Authors

The Gothic tradition

Mary Shelley — Frankenstein (1818)

The most philosophically ambitious Gothic text. Shelley uses Gothic conventions (a dark laboratory, a creature assembled from corpses, a pursuit across Arctic wastes) to explore questions of scientific ethics, social responsibility and what makes us human. The Gothic setting externalises the moral darkness of Victor's transgression. See: Mary Shelley profile

Later Gothic: Bram Stoker and Oscar Wilde

The Gothic revival in the 1880s-90s: Stevenson's Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde (1886) — the double; Wilde's The Picture of Dorian Gray (1890) — the portrait as Gothic device; Bram Stoker's Dracula (1897) — the vampire as Victorian sexual anxiety. The late Victorian Gothic reflects anxieties about sexuality, empire, degeneration and the fragility of the self.

Key Text

Mary Shelley — Frankenstein, Chapter V (1818)

"It was on a dreary night of November that I beheld the accomplishment of my toils. With an anxiety that almost amounted to agony, I collected the instruments of life around me, that I might infuse a spark of being into the lifeless thing that lay at my feet." — Mary Shelley, Frankenstein, Chapter V

Every Gothic convention is present: "dreary night," "instruments of life" (the transgressive laboratory), "lifeless thing" (the animated corpse). Victor's "anxiety that almost amounted to agony" — extreme emotion. "The spark of being" — the Promethean transgression. The passage creates horror not through the supernatural but through the violation of natural limits.
