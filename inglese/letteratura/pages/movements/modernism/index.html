The break

"Make it new" — the Modernist revolution

Modernism is the radical literary and artistic movement of roughly 1900–1940, characterised by a deliberate break with 19th-century conventions and a search for new forms adequate to the modern experience. Ezra Pound's slogan "Make it new" (1934) sums up its imperative. The causes: WWI (which destroyed belief in progress and civilisation), Freudian psychology (the irrational unconscious beneath rational behaviour), Einsteinian physics (no absolute frame of reference), industrial urbanisation, the crisis of empire — all producing a sense that the old certainties had collapsed and new forms were needed.

The year 1922 is "Annus Mirabilis" of Modernism: Joyce's Ulysses, Eliot's The Waste Land, Woolf's Jacob's Room and Rilke's Duino Elegies all published. The movement had been building since the 1890s but exploded in this single year.

Key Techniques

How Modernism works

  • Stream of consciousness — interior monologue that attempts to reproduce the continuous flow of thought: associations, memories, sensory impressions, without authorial mediation. Coined by William James (1890); used by Woolf, Joyce, Faulkner
  • Free indirect discourse — the narrator inhabits the character's consciousness without using first person: "She would buy the flowers herself" (Woolf) — we cannot tell who speaks, narrator or character
  • Mythic method — using ancient myth as a structural skeleton beneath a modern narrative (Joyce's Homer, Eliot's Fisher King)
  • Fragmentation — no unified narrative voice, no linear chronology; multiple perspectives, discontinuous time
  • Allusion — dense intertextual reference to literature, history, philosophy; Eliot's notes to The Waste Land as self-parody
  • The epiphany — Joyce's term: a sudden revelation that illuminates the ordinary with extraordinary significance

The Modernist novel vs the Victorian novel

Omniscient narrator → Multiple perspectives
No single authoritative voice
Linear chronology → Psychological time
Time is how the mind experiences it
Social panorama → Interior consciousness
The self, not society, is the subject
Readable style → Difficulty
Form mirrors the fragmented modern mind
Resolution → Open ending
No tidy moral conclusion
Key Authors

The Modernist canon

Virginia Woolf (1882–1941)

Free indirect discourse, stream of consciousness, Bergson's durée vs clock time. Mrs Dalloway (1925) — a single June day in London, two parallel narratives (Clarissa Dalloway and Septimus Warren Smith), Big Ben as the measuring clock vs the fluidity of inner life. See: Virginia Woolf profile

James Joyce (1882–1941)

From the plain style of Dubliners to the total linguistic experiment of Finnegans Wake. Ulysses (1922): one day in Dublin (16 June 1904), 18 episodes, each with a different style, structured on Homer's Odyssey. Molly Bloom's final 40-page soliloquy — unpunctuated interior monologue — is the most technically radical passage in English prose. See: James Joyce profile

"On or about December 1910, human character changed." — Virginia Woolf, Mr Bennett and Mrs Brown (1924)
