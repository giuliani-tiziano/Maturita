Historical Context

Queen Victoria's Britain (1837–1901)

The Victorian period is defined by the reign of Queen Victoria (1837–1901) — an age of extraordinary paradoxes. On one hand: the height of the British Empire, rapid industrialisation, technological innovation (railways, telegraph, photography, Darwin), vast accumulation of wealth, rising middle class. On the other: brutal child labour, grinding urban poverty, radical economic inequality, the exploitation of empire, the repression of sexuality and dissent. Victorian literature is largely about the tension between these two realities.

The Victorian Compromise

Public morality and private reality

Victorian respectability

The public code of Victorian respectability demanded: sexual chastity (especially female), religious observance, hard work, domestic virtue, sobriety, charity. The "angel in the house" (Coventry Patmore's phrase) was the Victorian ideal of femininity: selfless, pure, domestic. The public sphere — commerce, empire, politics — was male; the domestic sphere — home, children, morality — was female.

The gap beneath

Beneath this respectable surface: widespread prostitution in every major city; the systematic exploitation of children in factories and mines; opium addiction among the middle classes; imperial violence covered over by the language of "civilisation." Victorian literature is largely an exposure of this gap — Dickens's novels, Hardy's tragedies, Wilde's comedies all attack the discrepancy between Victorian ideals and Victorian reality.

The Novel

Victorian fiction — the age of the novel

The Victorian period is the golden age of the English novel. Serialisation (monthly and weekly instalments in magazines like Household Words, All the Year Round) created a new mass reading public and a new relationship between author and audience. Dickens could respond to reader letters; kill characters before their time if unpopular; extend those who were loved. The novel became the central literary form because it could hold both social panorama and individual interiority.

Major novelists: Charles Dickens (social satire, child victims), George Eliot (psychological realism, moral seriousness), Thomas Hardy (rural tragedy, Fate and the individual), William Thackeray (satirical social panorama), Elizabeth Gaskell (industrial novels).

Darwin and the crisis of faith

Charles Darwin's On the Origin of Species (1859) was the most destabilising intellectual event of the Victorian period. If humans evolved from earlier species through natural selection — not created by God — then the Bible was not literally true, the moral order was not divinely guaranteed, and humanity was not the pinnacle of Creation but one species among many. This crisis of faith runs through Victorian literature: Tennyson's In Memoriam, Matthew Arnold's "Dover Beach" ("the Sea of Faith / Was once, too, at the full"), Hardy's fiction.

Key Text

Matthew Arnold — "Dover Beach" (1867)

"The Sea of Faith
Was once, too, at the full, and round earth's shore
Lay like the folds of a bright girdle furl'd.
But now I only hear
Its melancholy, long, withdrawing roar." — Matthew Arnold, Dover Beach

The retreating tide as a metaphor for the retreat of Christian faith — Darwinism and modern science have drained the sea of belief. Arnold's poem is the lament of the Victorian intellectual who has lost faith but cannot find a replacement: "we are here as on a darkling plain / Swept with confused alarms of struggle and flight." The world has become "a waste land" — Eliot would use the same metaphor sixty years later.
