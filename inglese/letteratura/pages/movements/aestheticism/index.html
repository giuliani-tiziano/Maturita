The doctrine

"Art for Art's Sake" — l'art pour l'art

Aestheticism was a late 19th-century movement that held beauty and aesthetic experience to be the supreme values — superior to moral, social or political utility. The slogan "Art for Art's Sake" (l'art pour l'art) originated in French philosophy (Théophile Gautier, 1835) and was developed in England by Walter Pater and Oscar Wilde. The central claim: art does not need to be morally useful, socially educational or politically committed. The only obligation of art is to be beautiful.

This was a direct challenge to Victorian utilitarianism (which valued things for their use) and to the Victorian moral novel (which required literature to teach). Aestheticism was, at heart, a refusal of Victorian values — an aristocratic, sensuous, artificial alternative to the earnest, productive, moralistic mainstream.

Key Figures

Pater, Whistler, and Wilde

Walter Pater (1839–1894)

Oxford critic and essayist; Studies in the History of the Renaissance (1873) — the theoretical bible of Aestheticism. "All art constantly aspires towards the condition of music" — art at its purest has no content separate from its form. The famous "Conclusion": "to burn always with this hard, gem-like flame, to maintain this ecstasy, is success in life." The purpose of life is the aesthetic experience itself.

Oscar Wilde (1854–1900)

The ultimate aesthete — theorist, playwright, wit and martyr. His Preface to The Picture of Dorian Gray (1890) is the English Aesthetic manifesto: "There is no such thing as a moral or an immoral book. Books are well written, or badly written. That is all." The portrait of Dorian Gray is the darkest implication of Aestheticism: the pursuit of pure beauty and pleasure without moral consequence leads to hidden corruption. See: Oscar Wilde profile

The Dandy

The Aesthete as social type

The Dandy — the man who dedicates his life to elegance, wit, and the cultivation of beauty — was the social embodiment of Aesthetic values. Beau Brummell (1778–1840) was the original; Byron romanticised him; Wilde perfected him. The Dandy refuses productive work, conventional ambition, and bourgeois morality. His only occupation is the refinement of taste and the production of brilliant conversation.

Fin-de-siècle and Decadence

The French phrase "fin-de-siècle" (end of the century) described the mood of the 1890s: exhaustion, decadence, morbidity, the sense that civilisation was ending. The Decadent movement (connected to but distinct from Aestheticism) embraced artificial over natural, the corrupt over the innocent, the exotic over the familiar. Wilde, Beardsley (illustrator), Ernest Dowson, Lionel Johnson — a brief, brilliant, tragic moment.

Key Text

Wilde — Preface to The Picture of Dorian Gray (1890)

"The artist is the creator of beautiful things. To reveal art and conceal the artist is art's aim. The critic is he who can translate into another manner or a new material his impression of beautiful things. The highest as the lowest form of criticism is a mode of autobiography. Those who find ugly meanings in beautiful things are corrupt without being charming. This is a fault. Those who find beautiful meanings in beautiful things are the cultivated. For these there is hope. They are the elect to whom beautiful things mean only Beauty." — Oscar Wilde, Preface to The Picture of Dorian Gray
