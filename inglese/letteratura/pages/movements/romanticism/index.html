Historical Context

The Age of Revolutions (1785–1830)

Romanticism is a literary, artistic and intellectual movement that arose as a reaction against Enlightenment rationalism and the Industrial Revolution. Its immediate context: the American Revolution (1776), the French Revolution (1789), and the Industrial Revolution — three events that simultaneously promised liberation and produced terror, upheaval and new forms of oppression. The Romantic poets responded with a radical privileging of imagination, feeling, individuality and nature over reason, order, convention and the machine.

Two Generations

First and Second Generation Romantics

First Generation (1785–1810)

William Blake (1757–1827) — visionary prophet; Songs of Innocence and Experience, "mind-forg'd manacles"

William Wordsworth (1770–1850) — nature as moral teacher; Lyrical Ballads (1798), "emotion recollected in tranquillity"

Samuel Taylor Coleridge (1772–1834) — supernatural, opium, imagination; The Rime of the Ancient Mariner, "Kubla Khan"

The Lyrical Ballads (1798) is the founding document: Wordsworth's preface argues for "the real language of men" and emotion over artifice.

Second Generation (1810–1830)

Lord Byron (1788–1824) — the Byronic hero; Childe Harold, Don Juan; exile, political rebellion, death in Greece

Percy Bysshe Shelley (1792–1822) — radical politics, Prometheus as hero; Prometheus Unbound, "Ode to the West Wind"

John Keats (1795–1821) — beauty, mortality, negative capability; Ode to a Nightingale, Ode on a Grecian Urn

The Second Generation were shorter-lived, more radical, and died (or left England) before 30.

Key Ideas

The Romantic worldview

  • Imagination — the highest human faculty; more than mere fantasy, the creative power that perceives truth
  • Nature — not a background but a moral and spiritual teacher; the sublime landscape as access to the divine
  • Individualism — the unique self, the solitary genius, the poet as prophet and social outsider
  • Liberty — political and personal freedom; opposition to tyranny (Napoleonic, British, social)
  • The Middle Ages — the gothic past, the folk ballad, the national legend as alternatives to classical models
  • Childhood and nature — the child closer to truth than the adult corrupted by civilisation

Romanticism vs Neoclassicism

Reason → Imagination
Emotion and creativity over logic
Order → Freedom
Spontaneous form over strict classical structure
Society → Individual
The solitary genius over the social type
The city → Nature
The wild landscape over the classical garden
The present → The past
Medieval nostalgia over classical Rome/Greece
Key Text

Wordsworth's Preface to Lyrical Ballads (1800) — the Romantic manifesto

"Poetry is the spontaneous overflow of powerful feelings: it takes its origin from emotion recollected in tranquillity." — Wordsworth, Preface to Lyrical Ballads (1800)

The four key claims of the Preface: (1) poetry uses the "real language of men"; (2) subjects come from "incidents and situations from common life"; (3) emotion is "recollected in tranquillity" — not immediate but remembered; (4) the purpose is to trace "the primary laws of our nature." These four principles define Romantic poetry and distinguish it from 18th-century Neoclassicism.
