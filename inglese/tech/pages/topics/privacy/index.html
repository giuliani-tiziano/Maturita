Topic 18 · Society & Employment

Privacy, Ethics & GDPR

Data Protection · GDPR Rights · Net Neutrality · Intellectual Property · Digital Ethics

Overview

Why does digital privacy matter?

Every time you use a website, app or digital service, you generate data — about your location, preferences, behaviour and identity. Digital privacy concerns who collects this data, how it is used, and what rights you have over it. Privacy is not only an individual concern: mass data collection by corporations and governments raises fundamental questions about democracy, autonomy and power.

Regulation

GDPR — General Data Protection Regulation

What is GDPR?

The GDPR (EU, effective May 2018) is the world's most comprehensive data protection law. It applies to any organisation that processes personal data of EU residents — regardless of where the organisation is based.

Personal data = any information that can identify a person: name, email, IP address, location, cookie ID, biometric data.

Your rights under GDPR

  • Right to be informed — organisations must say how they use your data.
  • Right of access — you can request a copy of all data held about you.
  • Right to erasure — the "right to be forgotten": request deletion of your data.
  • Right to rectification — correct inaccurate data.
  • Right to data portability — receive your data in a usable format.
  • Right to object — object to processing for direct marketing.

GDPR key principles

  • Lawfulness, fairness, transparency
  • Purpose limitation — data collected for a specific purpose cannot be reused for another.
  • Data minimisation — collect only what is necessary.
  • Accuracy — keep data up to date.
  • Storage limitation — don't keep data longer than necessary.
  • Integrity and confidentiality — secure data against breaches.

Penalties: up to €20 million or 4% of global annual turnover (whichever is higher).

Broader Issues

Net neutrality & intellectual property

Net neutrality

Net neutrality is the principle that internet service providers (ISPs) must treat all internet traffic equally — they cannot slow down, block, or charge more for specific websites or services. Without net neutrality, ISPs could charge more for Netflix than for their own streaming service, or block competitors entirely.

Intellectual property in the digital world

  • Copyright — automatic protection of creative works (code, text, images, music) from reproduction without permission.
  • Patent — protects inventions/processes for up to 20 years.
  • Open source — software whose source code is freely available (GPL, MIT licences).
  • Creative Commons — flexible licences allowing creators to specify how their work can be reused.
