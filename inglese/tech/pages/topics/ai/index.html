Artificial Intelligence & Machine Learning

Turing Test · ML Types · Neural Networks · Ethics & Bias

Overview

What is Artificial Intelligence?

Artificial Intelligence (AI) is the field of computer science concerned with creating systems capable of performing tasks that normally require human intelligence — reasoning, learning, problem-solving, perception and language understanding. AI is not new (the term was coined in 1956) but recent advances in computing power and data availability have transformed it from a research curiosity into a technology reshaping every industry.

History

From the Turing Test to ChatGPT

1950
The Turing Test — "Can machines think?"
Alan Turing proposed the "imitation game": if a machine can converse in a way indistinguishable from a human, it can be said to "think." The Turing Test remains the most famous benchmark in AI — though many argue it tests deception, not intelligence.
1956
Dartmouth Conference — AI is born as a field
John McCarthy coins the term "Artificial Intelligence." The optimism was enormous — researchers predicted human-level AI within 20 years. The first "AI winter" (lack of funding and progress) followed in the 1970s.
1997
Deep Blue defeats Kasparov at chess
IBM's Deep Blue became the first computer to defeat a world chess champion under standard conditions. A landmark moment — but Deep Blue used brute-force search, not learning.
2012
Deep Learning revolution — ImageNet
AlexNet, a deep convolutional neural network, dramatically outperforms all other systems at the ImageNet visual recognition challenge. The deep learning era begins: neural networks with many layers, trained on massive datasets.
2022–
Large Language Models — ChatGPT, GPT-4, Gemini
Generative AI reaches mainstream adoption. Large Language Models (LLMs) trained on vast text corpora can write, code, translate, and reason. Ethical debates about misinformation, copyright, job displacement and AI safety intensify.
Core Concepts

Types of Machine Learning

Three main approaches

  • Supervised learning — the model is trained on labelled data (inputs paired with correct outputs). Examples: image classification, spam detection, credit scoring.
  • Unsupervised learning — the model finds patterns in unlabelled data. Examples: customer segmentation, anomaly detection, recommendation systems.
  • Reinforcement learning — an agent learns by trial and error, receiving rewards for good actions. Examples: game-playing AI (AlphaGo), robotics, self-driving cars.

Neural networks

Inspired by the human brain, a neural network consists of layers of nodes (neurons) connected by weighted links. Data passes forward through the layers; the network learns by adjusting weights to minimise errors (backpropagation).

  • Input layer — receives raw data
  • Hidden layers — extract patterns (more layers = "deeper")
  • Output layer — produces the result

Deep learning = neural networks with many hidden layers.

Ethics

Ethical concerns — AI and society

Key ethical issues

  • Algorithmic bias — AI trained on biased data reproduces and amplifies discrimination (facial recognition failing on darker skin tones; hiring algorithms disadvantaging women).
  • Accountability — when an AI system causes harm, who is responsible? The developer? The user? The company?
  • Privacy — AI systems often require vast amounts of personal data for training.
  • Job displacement — automation threatens repetitive jobs; new jobs emerge but the transition is unequal.
  • Deepfakes — AI-generated synthetic media enabling disinformation and identity fraud.
