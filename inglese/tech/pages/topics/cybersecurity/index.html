Topic 11 · Security

Cybersecurity & Threats

CIA Triad · Malware · Social Engineering · Defence Strategies

Overview

What is cybersecurity?

Cybersecurity is the practice of protecting computer systems, networks and data from digital attacks, theft and damage. As society becomes increasingly dependent on digital infrastructure — from banking and healthcare to critical national infrastructure — cybersecurity has become one of the most important fields in technology.

Core Concept

The CIA Triad

The three pillars of information security

Every cybersecurity decision is measured against the CIA Triad:

  • Confidentiality — data is accessible only to authorised users. Tools: encryption, access controls, passwords.
  • Integrity — data is accurate and cannot be modified without authorisation. Tools: hashing, digital signatures, audit logs.
  • Availability — systems and data are accessible when needed. Threats: DDoS attacks, hardware failure, ransomware.
"Security is a process, not a product."
— Bruce Schneier, security expert

Social engineering attacks

Many successful attacks exploit people, not technology:

  • Phishing — fraudulent emails/websites mimicking trusted sources to steal credentials or install malware.
  • Spear phishing — targeted phishing using personal information about the victim.
  • Pretexting — creating a fabricated scenario to manipulate someone into revealing information.
  • Vishing — voice phishing: phone calls impersonating banks, IT support etc.
  • Baiting — leaving infected USB drives in public places.
Defence

Defence strategies

Technical defences

  • Firewall — monitors and filters incoming/outgoing network traffic based on rules.
  • Antivirus/antimalware — detects and removes known malicious software.
  • Encryption — makes data unreadable without the correct key.
  • VPN (Virtual Private Network) — encrypts internet traffic, hiding it from ISPs and attackers.
  • Two-factor authentication (2FA) — requires a second proof of identity (code, fingerprint).
  • Patching — keeping software updated to fix known vulnerabilities.

Exam tips — Cybersecurity

Key points to remember for the exam:

  • Always define CIA before discussing specific threats.
  • Distinguish malware types by how they spread (self-replicating worm vs user-triggered virus).
  • Give real examples: WannaCry (2017 ransomware), Morris Worm (1988), Stuxnet (2010).
  • Link social engineering to the idea that humans are the weakest link.
  • Mention GDPR when discussing data breaches — legal obligations for companies.
